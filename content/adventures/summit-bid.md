Some people climb mountains because they're there. I climb mountains because the chart says ALL-TIME HIGH and I am not the kind of dinosaur who waits at base camp eating yak butter while the move goes without me.

May 29, 1953. The roof of the world. 29,032 feet. The biggest unbroken resistance level on the planet.

I had a hat. I had a rope. I had Tenzing, the calmest sherpa in the entire Himalayas. And I had a moon-shaped problem named Moony, who was somewhere in the icefall below screaming about avalanches.

## Chapter 1: Base Camp Bears

Base camp at 17,600 feet smelled like wet rope and burned tea. Moony was already there when I arrived, holding a clipboard and a face like a bad earnings print.

"You can't climb it, Dippy," he said. "The chart goes vertical. It's parabolic. It's unsustainable. Every previous attempt ended in a drawdown of negative one hundred percent."

"Moony," I said, "those climbers died. That isn't a drawdown. That is a different chart entirely."

"It's the same chart!" he hissed. "Risk-adjusted, you are taking infinite downside for finite altitude!"

I looked at him. Then I looked up. The summit was somewhere above the clouds, hidden behind a plume of snow that the locals called *the banner*. The mountain was breathing. The mountain was alive. The mountain was a clean breakout setup with confirmed volume.

"I have stops," I said. "I have oxygen. I have a partner who doesn't speak fear. And I have a thesis."

"WHAT THESIS?"

"That the top is up there," I said, "and I am going to stand on it."

## Chapter 2: The Khumbu Icefall

The Khumbu Icefall is a frozen waterfall the size of a small country. It moves three feet a day. It eats people. It is the choppiest, ugliest pre-market session you have ever crossed, and you have to cross it before sunrise because once the sun hits, the whole thing starts cracking like a margin call.

Tenzing led. I followed. We crossed crevasses on aluminum ladders laid flat across nothing. One foot wrong, you're a position closed at the open of forever.

Halfway across the third ladder, I heard a noise. Not the mountain. Not the wind. Something whimpering.

I looked down into the crevasse.

Moony.

He had followed us. He had followed us to argue, and he had fallen in. He was wedged about twenty feet down, complaining that the ice was *unprofessional*.

"Throw me a rope!" he yelled. "I demand a rope!"

"Why are you here, Moony?"

"To stop you from making a terrible decision!"

"And how did that work out?"

Tenzing, who had been silent the whole climb, leaned over the edge. He looked at Moony. He looked at me. He looked at Moony again.

"He'll be fine," Tenzing said, very calmly. "He's the size of a small moon. He has support."

We left him a granola bar and kept climbing.

## Chapter 3: The Death Zone

Above 26,000 feet, the air thins to about a third of sea level. Your body starts dying. Your brain stops making good decisions. Climbers call it *the death zone*. Traders call it *the final hour on a melt-up Friday*. They are the same thing.

This is where you find out who you are. Most people, up here, panic. They cut winners. They oversize. They forget where they put their oxygen.

I did none of those things. I had my stops. My stops were the ridge line on the right — go past those and you're falling 8,000 feet into Tibet. My target was up. My size was *me*. My time horizon was *now*.

We climbed in the dark. Crampons biting. Ice axe swinging. Step. Breathe. Step. Breathe. Each step a tick on the tape.

Then the sky went pink. Then orange. Then gold.

And the slope ran out of slope.

We were on top.

The whole world was below us. India to the south, looking like a chart on a screen. Tibet to the north, looking like the same chart upside down. Wind. Sun. Nothing higher.

Tenzing planted the flag. I planted a green one next to it. Mine said *ATH*. He smiled. He didn't ask what it meant. He knew.

We took the picture. We took two pictures. Then we turned around, because the summit isn't the trade. The summit is the entry. The trade is getting home.

## Chapter 4: The Walk Down

Going down a mountain is harder than going up. The exits are where people die. The exits are where positions die.

We descended in clean steps. We watched our footing. We didn't celebrate. We didn't post on socials. We didn't ladder up into another 8,000m peak on momentum. We took the win, and we took it back to base camp, and we drank the burned tea.

Moony was waiting for us at Camp 2. Someone had pulled him out of the crevasse. He was wrapped in three blankets and his nose was bright red.

"You made it," he said, which was as close to a compliment as he had ever managed.

"We made it. Did you summit?"

"I — I held my position. At my level. I think that's the smarter play, statistically. The summit is just a number. It's a *vanity* metric. It's not real."

"Moony," I said, "I stood on it."

"Well, it didn't count, because you didn't post it."

I held up the photograph.

Moony stared. His grey face went slightly more grey. He opened his mouth to say something clever, and the wind took his clipboard and threw it into the icefall, and he chased it, and he fell into a different crevasse.

The mountain was breathing. The mountain was alive. The mountain knew.

I drank my tea.

All-time high. Confirmed.
