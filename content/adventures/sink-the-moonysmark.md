## Chapter 1: Operation Rheinpump

The signal came at dawn. Reuters terminal flashing yellow:

**MOONY SLIPS PORT AT GDYNIA. HEADING WEST. ARMED AND OVER-LEVERED.**

Translation: he'd bought 90,000 shares of a Norwegian fjord ETF on 50-to-1 margin and was steaming into the Atlantic looking for shipping lanes to torpedo. The *Moonysmark*, his pride and joy — eight 15-inch turrets, every one of them loaded with weekly call options, and a balance sheet so big it bent the chart axis.

I was eating fish-and-chips on HMS Dippy when the dispatch from Whitehall came through.

**SINK THE MOONYSMARK.**

I spat out a chip. "Already? Market only opened forty minutes ago."

## Chapter 2: HMS Hood Goes Down

We caught him in the Denmark Strait. HMS Hood — my line in the sand, my support at $4.20, the pride of the British Bid — opened the engagement at 26,000 yards.

It lasted eight minutes.

A salvo from the *Moonysmark* punched straight through the deck and detonated the magazine. A column of smoke a mile high. 1,415 longs lost. My P&L blotter glowed red the colour of a Tuesday open after a hawkish Fed.

I watched her go down through the spotter scope and felt the cold thing in my chest that every trader feels exactly once: *the level didn't hold.*

Moony's signal officer broadcast in clear, on every channel, in seven languages:

**"GENTLEMEN. BUY THE BREAKOUT."**

## Chapter 3: The Chase

I called in everything.

Force H from Gibraltar — the London algo desk, twelve quants in cardigans, vicious. Tribal-class destroyers — small-cap momentum scalpers who would hunt anything that moved. The carrier *Ark Royal*, decks lined with the slowest, ugliest, most outdated aircraft the Admiralty owned.

Moony thought he'd escaped. He turned south for Brest — that French port of consolidation, low-volume sideways grind, the cemetery where bag-holders go to spend retirement holding bags. He was already drafting the tweet. *Champagne photo. Cigar. "Position trimmed. Still bullish."*

He had not noticed the biplanes.

## Chapter 4: The Rudder Strike

From the carrier *Ark Royal*, three squadrons of Fairey Swordfish lumbered off the deck into a forty-knot headwind. Canvas-and-wire biplanes carrying torpedoes that the *Moonysmark*'s gunners openly laughed at.

These were my lagging indicators. The 200-day moving average. The MACD that prints three bars after the move is already cold. The volume-weighted average price that turns up to the trade like a friend who texts "omw" two hours ago.

Moony's anti-aircraft fire couldn't elevate fast enough to hit them — the Swordfish flew slower than the shells' minimum tracking speed. They came in low. Released. Banked.

One torpedo. Just one. Hit the rudder.

The *Moonysmark*'s helm locked hard to port. From that moment on she could only steer in a tight, perfect circle, nose into the prevailing wind — which happened to be a thirty-knot bearish gust blowing straight into the muzzles of my main battery.

## Chapter 5: The Final Salvo

HMS King George V opened up at 16,000 yards. HMS Rodney followed. Then HMS Dippy turned her screws and rolled in close enough to read the rivets.

Moony crouched in his armoured conning tower, screaming into a stuck microphone:

**"BUY THE DIP. BUY THE DIP. BUY THE D—"**

The chart sank at 10:39 AM. Position size: zero. Margin call: total. Equity wiped clean to the waterline and then below it. The Atlantic shipping lanes — every dollar of float in three sectors — were mine.

I lit a cigar on the bridge. The Channel was calm. Volume was light. The trend was my friend.

Moony's last transmission gargled up out of a flooding signal room, garbled by water and self-pity:

**"...rotated... to... value..."**

He always says that. Right before he goes under.
