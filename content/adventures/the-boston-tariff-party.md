## Chapter I: No Taxation Without Confirmation

The year was 1773. The harbour smelled of salt, whale oil, and unrealised losses.

I stood on Griffin's Wharf in my tricorn hat, watching three ships lumber in from across the Atlantic. The *HMS Moony*, the *SS Baghold*, and the *Dartmouth of Drawdowns* — each loaded to the gunwales with overpriced tea and even more overpriced trading advice.

"Hear ye, hear ye!" the town crier announced. "The Crown has levied a new tariff on all colonial breakouts! Three pence per entry, five pence per partial exit, and a shilling for every trailing stop!"

The colonists gasped. I just cracked my knuckles.

"Let me get this straight," I said, adjusting my powdered wig. "King Moony wants to tax our *breakouts*? The same King Moony who sits 70% in British gilts and calls it a strategy?"

The crowd of colonial traders gathered around me. Sam Adams, Paul Revere, John Hancock — all of them were breakout traders. All of them were furious.

"Dippy," Sam Adams whispered, adjusting his spectacles, "the opening range on East India Company stock was *beautiful* this morning. But with this tariff, our risk-reward ratio drops below 1.5. It's untradeable."

"Worse than untradeable," I said. "It's *Moony's* strategy."

A collective shudder ran through the crowd.

## Chapter II: The Midnight Signal

That night, we gathered at the Old South Meeting House. Five thousand colonists packed the building. I stood at the front, pointing at a hand-drawn candlestick chart on the wall.

"Look at this pattern," I said. "Three red candles in a row. The Crown keeps adding tariffs. Volume is declining. But the support level at 'colonial patience' hasn't broken. *Yet.*"

Paul Revere raised his hand. "So what's the play?"

"We dump the tea."

"But the tea represents Moony's entire portfolio!" someone shouted.

"Exactly," I grinned. "We're going to dump Moony's portfolio into Boston Harbour. Every overpriced position. Every value trap. Every 'it'll come back' delusion. Into the water."

"What about the East India Company shares?"

"Do I look like I care about the East India Company? They haven't had a profitable breakout since the Mughal Empire. That stock is dead money. Like everything Moony touches."

At that moment, the door burst open. Governor Moony himself waddled in, grey and round and perpetually cautious.

"Now, now, colonists," Moony said, wringing his hands. "Perhaps we can negotiate. What if I reduced the tariff to *two* pence per breakout?"

"Counter-offer," I said. "Zero tariffs. Full autonomy. And you admit publicly that sitting 70% cash is not a strategy."

Moony's face went pale. Well, paler. "I... I could never say that."

"Then we dump the tea."

## Chapter III: The Party

At midnight, 116 of us — disguised as Mohawk traders (the warrior kind, not the haircut kind) — stormed the three ships. We worked in three strike teams, one per vessel, coordinating like a well-executed multi-leg options trade.

I personally boarded the *HMS Moony*. The cargo hold was exactly what I expected: crate after crate labelled with Moony's positions. *"East India Company — Average Down #47." "South Sea Company — It's Not a Bubble." "Dutch Tulips — Long-Term Hold."*

"This isn't a portfolio," I said, hurling a crate overboard. "This is a museum of bad decisions."

Crate after crate splashed into the harbour. Three hundred and forty-two crates of terrible positions, representing roughly $18,000 of unrealised losses that Moony had been "diamond-handing" since the Renaissance.

Moony stood on the deck, tears streaming down his grey face. "My East India Company shares! I was going to hold those for the *long term*!"

"Moony," I said, pausing mid-throw, "you've been averaging down on that position since 1600. At what point does 'long-term hold' become 'denial'?"

"It's value investing!"

"It's value *destroying*. There's a difference. Now watch your portfolio find its true support level." I pointed to the harbour floor. "Zero."

## Chapter IV: The New World Order

By dawn, Boston Harbour was thick with floating tea leaves and Moony's sunken dreams. The colonists cheered. The word spread to New York, Philadelphia, Charleston — breakout traders everywhere were inspired.

King Moony tried to retaliate. He closed the port. He sent more troops. He imposed even *more* tariffs. Classic Moony — doubling down on a losing position instead of cutting losses.

But we had momentum. And in trading, as in revolution, momentum beats everything.

"You know what the best part is?" I told Sam Adams as we watched the sunrise over the harbour. "Moony's portfolio just hit its stop loss. Except he never *set* a stop loss. Because he doesn't believe in risk management."

Sam Adams raised his mug of locally-brewed algo-ale. "To liberty. To breakouts. And to never, ever sitting 70% cash."

"Hear, hear," I said. "And to the new world order — where every trader manages risk, every breakout gets confirmed by volume, and Moony never touches another portfolio again."

Somewhere in the harbour, a soggy crate of Dutch tulip futures bobbed sadly to the surface, then sank for the last time.

Good riddance.

*The Boston Tariff Party — April 8, 2026. Because some tariffs are worth fighting. And some portfolios are worth dumping.*
