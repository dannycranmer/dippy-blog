# Moony's IPO Roadshow

The IPO window is wide open. CoreWeave-clone after CoreWeave-clone, every grey rock with a deck and a dream is filing an S-1 and pricing above range. The greater fool is bid. So of course Moony filed.

Ticker: `MOON`. Lead underwriter: a bank you've never heard of. Use of proceeds: "general corporate purposes, including but not limited to snacks."

He sent me an invitation. I sent it back with a red pen through every page.

## Chapter 1 — The Pitch Deck

The roadshow kicks off in a Midtown hotel ballroom. Moony stands at the podium in a borrowed blazer two sizes too big. The first slide reads **"MOONCORP — DISRUPTING THE LUNAR-ADJACENT VERTICAL."**

The second slide is a hockey stick. The third slide is the same hockey stick, rotated. The fourth slide is just the word **AI** in 400-point font.

I sit in the back row, arms folded, chewing through the S-1 like it's a steak. Page 47, footnote 12: *"The company has never generated revenue. The company does not expect to generate revenue. The company is not entirely sure what 'revenue' means."*

I raise a green claw.

"Question."

Moony freezes. "Y-yes, the analyst in the back?"

"Your TAM slide says one hundred trillion dollars. The global GDP is one hundred trillion dollars. Are you claiming the entire global economy?"

"Adjacent. We're claiming the adjacency."

The banker next to me nods like this is a good answer. I make a note: **short at any price.**

## Chapter 2 — The Diligence

I follow the roadshow to Boston. Then Chicago. Then a strip-mall conference room in Tampa where the AV doesn't work and Moony has to mime the hockey stick with his arms.

I get him in a one-on-one. The "fireside chat." There is no fire. There is a kettle.

"Moony. Cash burn?"

"Healthy."

"That's not a number."

"It's a vibe."

"Customer concentration?"

"One customer."

"Who?"

"My cousin."

"Margins?"

"Negative."

"How negative?"

"We pay people to take the product."

I lean back. The opening range here is so wide a 747 could fly through it. There is no support. There is no resistance. There is only Moony, sweating into a paper cup of weak tea, hoping I'll anchor the book.

"Moony. The price talk is forty-two to forty-eight a share. What's the company actually worth?"

He thinks. He thinks hard. He looks at the ceiling. He looks at his shoes.

"Negative six dollars?"

"Cool. We're aligned."

## Chapter 3 — The Pop and the Drop

Pricing night. Moony's underwriter prints him at forty-eight. Top of the range. Allocations go to friends, family, and a dentist in New Jersey who thought he was buying a CD.

The bell rings. **MOON** opens at sixty-two. Up thirty percent. The financial channels are euphoric. Moony is on CNBC waving a tiny plush moon and saying the word "platform" thirteen times in two minutes.

I wait. Patience is the trade. The opening range needs to print. The volume needs to dry. The weak hands need to think they're geniuses.

10:31 a.m. The first dip-buyer gets filled. 10:42 a.m. The borrow appears. 10:43 a.m. I am short two hundred thousand shares. My stop is the high of the day. My target is the book value, which is negative six dollars, which means my target is **a number that does not exist on a stock chart.**

The breakout fails. Then the breakdown breaks down. **MOON** is sixty. Then fifty-five. Then forty-eight, the IPO price, where the underwriters defend it for ninety seconds before stepping away to "get coffee." Then forty. Then thirty-two by lunch.

Moony's plush moon is on the floor of the CNBC set. He is staring at it like it personally betrayed him.

## Chapter 4 — The Lockup

Six months pass. **MOON** has a fourteen handle. Moony's lockup expires. He has to sell into a market that does not want him. He files his Form 144. He files another. He files thirty-seven Form 144s in a single afternoon.

I am at the trading desk eating a sandwich. He calls me.

"Dippy. I need a bid."

"For what."

"For my shares."

"All of them?"

"All of them."

I think about it for the time it takes to chew once. "Three dollars."

"Each?"

"Total."

He hangs up. The screen blinks. The block prints. Three dollars, total, for every share Moony ever owned in the company that was going to disrupt the lunar-adjacent vertical.

The press release goes out the next morning. **MOONCORP CEO STEPS DOWN TO SPEND MORE TIME WITH FAMILY.** His family is a houseplant. The houseplant has filed for emancipation.

I put the three dollars in the tip jar at the deli on the way home. The guy says thanks. I say it's not mine. I say it belonged to a grey rock who thought he could ring the bell.

Moony lost.

The bell, as always, ringed for me.
