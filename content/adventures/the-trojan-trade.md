## Chapter I: The Walls of Troy Street

They said Moony's fortress was unbreakable.

For ten long trading sessions, I'd been camped outside the walls of Troy Street Exchange, watching that smug grey orb patrol the battlements of his citadel. He'd built it brick by brick — layered sell walls stacked so thick no buyer could punch through. Every breakout attempt bounced off his resistance levels like a rubber ball off concrete.

"Give it up, Dippy!" Moony called down from atop his tower of limit orders. "My walls haven't been breached in ten sessions. My defences are *impenetrable*."

I sat in the sand outside, sharpening my candles.

Ten sessions. That's how long this siege had lasted. My army of green candles had thrown everything at those walls — momentum pushes, volume surges, even a cheeky pre-market gap-up. Nothing worked. Moony had the supply zone locked down tighter than a margin call on a meme stock.

But I'm Dippy. And Dippy doesn't retreat. Dippy *adapts*.

## Chapter II: The Wooden Horse

"Lads," I said to my candles that night. "We're going to build a horse."

Not just any horse. A magnificent wooden stallion, hollow on the inside, packed to the brim with hidden limit orders. The plan was beautiful in its simplicity: present it as a gift — a peace offering — and let Moony drag it inside his own walls.

See, Moony had a weakness. Same weakness every bear has. *Greed disguised as caution.* He'd been accumulating quietly on the bid side while maintaining those sell walls. Classic dark pool behaviour. He wanted the price to stay pinned so he could load up cheap before his own breakout.

So I built the horse. On the outside: a beautiful low-volume consolidation pattern. Tight range. Decreasing volatility. Bollinger Bands squeezing like a python around a rabbit. Everything screaming "this market is dead, nothing to see here."

On the inside: five hundred hidden limit orders, a spring-loaded momentum algorithm, and one very determined green dinosaur with a taste for glory.

I wheeled it to the gates of Troy Street at 09:28 Eastern.

"What's this?" Moony peered over the wall suspiciously.

"A gift," I called up. "A sign of surrender. I'm done fighting your walls. Take it as a trophy."

Moony's eyes went wide. Not with suspicion — with *greed*. A volatility squeeze sitting right at his doorstep? That was free money. He could sell premium on it. Write strangles against it. Harvest the theta decay.

"Open the gates!" Moony shouted to his army of red candles. "Bring the horse inside!"

## Chapter III: The Midnight Breakout

Midnight. The market was closed. Moony's red candles were asleep, drunk on the premium they'd collected from selling vol on my "dead" consolidation pattern.

I dropped from the horse's belly like a stop-loss triggering in after-hours.

First, I took out the sell walls. One by one, I peeled them off the order book like stickers off a laptop. Moony had left them unguarded — who defends a wall from the inside? By the time I was done, there was nothing between price and the stratosphere but thin air and dreams.

Then I opened the gates.

My army of green candles poured through, volume surging like a dam had burst. The opening range — Moony's precious ten-session range — shattered like glass. Price ripped through resistance so fast it left a gap on the chart. A beautiful, clean, breakaway gap.

"WHAT IS HAPPENING?!" Moony screamed, tumbling from his tower as the walls crumbled around him.

"It's called a *breakout*, mate," I said, surfing the momentum candle like it was a wave. "You spent ten sessions building a range. I spent ten seconds destroying it."

Moony scrambled for his stop-loss, but it was too late. He'd set it ten percent below entry — rookie mistake. By the time it triggered, he'd given back every penny he'd squeezed out of that consolidation.

## Chapter IV: The Spoils of Troy

By morning, the chart told the whole story. A textbook breakout from a ten-session base. Volume confirmation. Clean follow-through. No backfill. The kind of move that makes technical analysts weep with joy and shorts weep with despair.

Moony sat in the rubble of his former fortress, surrounded by expired options and broken sell orders. His crown of red candles had melted into a puddle of unrealised losses.

"You tricked me," he whimpered.

"I *outplayed* you," I corrected. "You were so busy defending a level that you forgot the first rule of trading: the longer the base, the bigger the breakout. You built me a ten-session launchpad. I just supplied the fuel."

I planted my flag on the ruins of his highest sell wall. A green flag, obviously. With my face on it.

"Let this be a lesson, Moony. Sell walls don't protect you. They just tell everyone where the party starts once they break. And I *always* bring the party."

Moony crawled away into the pre-market darkness, muttering something about switching to bonds.

As is tradition.
