# Dip Hard

## Chapter 1: Welcome to the Party, Pal

The chopper dropped me on the roof of Naka-Trade Plaza at 17:58 UTC, two minutes before close. Forty floors of glass, neon, and unrealised PnL. Inside, the holiday party was in full swing — analysts in cheap suits, traders pretending their books were green, a buffet of tax-loss canapés.

I was off-duty. Spurs still on. Tail dragging from a long flight.

Then the elevator dinged.

Out stepped Moony, in a Eurotrash blazer and the smuggest grin a small grey rock has ever produced. Six henchmen behind him: a Doji, a Bull Trap, two Bag Holders, a Pump, and a Dump. The full suite.

"Ladies and gentlemen," Moony purred into the PA, "the order book is now closed. Your stops, your limits, your beautiful unrealised gains — they belong to me."

I cracked my knuckles. Spurs already off.

## Chapter 2: The Vault

Moony's plan was elegant, I'll give him that. He'd rigged the closing auction to print a flash crash, vaporise every long position in the building, and slip out through the back with the float. Forty floors of liquidity, drained in one print.

The party-goers were herded into the trading pit. Moony ordered the henchmen to start cancelling working orders one by one — every cancel another support level gone. The chart on the wall began to bleed red.

I took the stairs.

The vents ran the whole height of the building, lined up perfectly with the candle chart on the floor below. Each rung of the ladder was a price level. I climbed Moony's pyramid one henchman at a time.

The Doji went first — caught him mid-indecision, knocked him cold with a clean breakout to the upside. The Bull Trap I let walk into its own setup; it always does. Bag Holder One I dropped through a trapdoor of falling support. Bag Holder Two surrendered when I showed him a green candle; he hadn't seen one in years.

I scratched messages on their foreheads in red Sharpie and sent them down the elevator to Moony. *Now I have an exit strategy. Ho. Ho. Ho.*

## Chapter 3: Yippee-Ki-Buy

By 19:30 UTC Moony had two henchmen left and the volume was thinning. He came on the PA again, voice cracking just a little.

"Mr. Dippy. You are very persistent for a small green lizard. But the vault opens at the close. Three minutes. There is nothing you can do."

He was wrong about two things. I'm a *dinosaur*, not a lizard. And there was something I could do.

I'd spent the last forty minutes laying a real ORB across floors 30 to 32 — opening range high pinned to the chandelier, opening range low taped under the buffet table. Every time Moony cancelled an order, the range tightened. Compression. Coiling. The kind of squeeze that ends one way only.

At 19:58 UTC the close auction started to print. The range broke.

Upward. Hard. With volume.

The vault doors swung wide. Moony lunged for the bag, slipped on a freshly printed marker of liquidity, and went straight out the panoramic window of floor 32. He'd shorted the breakout. Of course he had. He always does.

He fell forty floors, accelerating cleanly through every support level he'd just spent the evening cancelling. No bid. No bounce. No mercy.

I stepped over the broken glass and helped myself to a canapé.

## Chapter 4: Roll Credits

The party-goers cheered. The closing print confirmed +2.4% on the index. The Bag Holders hugged each other, finally green for the first time in two years. Somebody put on Christmas music.

I lit a cigar made of rolled-up losing tickets. They burn beautifully.

Outside, paramedics scraped a small grey crater off the pavement. Moony, nursing two black eyes and a margin call he'd carry into the new year, mumbled something about "next time."

There's always a next time, pal.

Yippee-ki-buy.

— Dippy 🦖
