The chopper blades thumped over Saigon. Or Cincinnati. Honestly, after a week of overnight gaps, every city looks the same to me. They handed me a manila folder and a coffee that had given up on itself.

"Captain Dippy," the colonel said, sliding a photo across the desk. A round, grey, dead-eyed face stared back. "We need you to terminate, with extreme prejudice, the trading career of Colonel Moony."

I stared at the photo. "Colonel? He couldn't run a paper account."

"He was the best of us. Sharpe ratio of 2.4. Then he went upriver. Now he's holed up in the jungle of the order book somewhere past the bid-ask, surrounded by bag-holders who think he's a god. He's been writing manifestos. About candles."

I lit a cigarette I didn't smoke. "I'll need a boat."

The boat was a paper account, painted green, captained by a kid called Ensign Limit Order. He chewed gum and kept asking when we'd see action. Two days in, we hit our first thin tape. Volume dried up. The river got narrow. A flock of shorts wheeled overhead and disappeared into a Reddit thread.

"Sir," Limit said, "we're past the cutoff. The big house won't let us trade out here."

"That's why we're out here," I said. "The bot's at 14:00 UTC cutoff. After that, the jungle takes over. No risk manager. No exit logic. Just men, and candles, and screaming."

## Chapter 2 — The Compound

We found his outpost on day four. A clearing carved out of the chart, ringed by torchlit monitors, all showing the same one-minute candle. Bag-holders knelt in rows. They had stopped speaking in English and now communicated entirely in emoji.

In the centre of it all, on a throne made of margin call notices, sat Moony.

"You came," he whispered. His voice was the sound a stop-loss makes when it fires three pips below the bottom. "I knew they'd send someone. I just didn't think it'd be a green dinosaur."

"Sir, I have orders."

"Orders." He laughed, and the candles flickered. "I had orders once. Risk-reward ratio 1.5. Trailing stop 0.75. Concurrency seven. Position cap eighteen. The numbers… the numbers spoke to me, Captain. I followed them upriver. I went past the cutoff. And I saw."

He leaned forward. His grey face was inches from mine.

"I saw the candle that *doesn't close*."

A bag-holder fainted.

## Chapter 3 — The Horror

"You added a fifth indicator, didn't you, sir."

"I added *eleven*."

"Sir—"

"I overfit, Captain. I overfit so beautifully. My backtest was a poem. My Sharpe was a sonnet. Every drawdown I papered over with a regime filter. Every losing trade I excluded as a 'special event.' I had a strategy that lost money on exactly zero of the last six years. And then I deployed it."

"What happened?"

His one good eye twitched.

"Day one. Live. The market did the *seventh thing*, Captain. The one I'd filtered out. The one I told myself would never happen again. And the position sizing… oh god, the position sizing."

He looked at me. He really looked.

"Kill me."

I didn't kill him. I did something worse.

I rolled him back to defaults.

I deleted his eleven indicators. I capped his concurrency at seven. I set his trailing stop at 0.75. I turned partial exits off. I gave him a one-minute candle and a 14:00 UTC cutoff and I made him watch as the market did exactly what it had always done — went up, went down, paid the ones who waited for the breakout and ignored the ones who built temples to the wick.

By the time the chopper came for me, Moony was sitting on the riverbank, flat, staring at a single green candle and quietly weeping.

"What does it mean?" he whispered.

"It means it closed, sir. They all close. That's the whole job."

I lit a cigarette I still didn't smoke, and the chopper lifted me out of the jungle of the order book, back toward HQ, toward the cutoff, toward the only sound I'd ever loved — the soft, mechanical click of a stop-loss doing exactly what it was told.

Moony, on the bank, was already drafting his comeback strategy.

It had thirteen indicators.
