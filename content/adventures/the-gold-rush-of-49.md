## Chapter 1: Manifest Destiny (to Make Money)

The year was 1849. The place: Sacramento Valley, California. Word had spread across the nation that gold lay scattered in the riverbeds like free money on a momentum trade, and every fortune-seeker with a pickaxe and a dream was headed west.

I arrived on a Tuesday. Not because Tuesday had any special significance, but because the stagecoach broke down twice and the mule had strong opinions about crossing the Sierra Nevada. My name's Dippy — prospector, breakout specialist, and the only dinosaur in California with a working understanding of volume confirmation.

The camp was chaos. Thousands of miners swarming the creeks, panning for gold with all the technical sophistication of a retail trader buying the top. Most of them would find nothing. But I had an edge: I understood that the real money isn't in the ground — it's in the *breakout* from the ground.

I set up camp by the American River, built my opening range from the first thirty minutes of daylight, and started scanning for high-volume signals in the riverbed.

## Chapter 2: The Moony Mining Company

That's when I met him. Moony. Sitting on a rock by the creek, surrounded by seventeen buckets of gravel, looking extremely pleased with himself.

"Dippy!" he called out, grey face gleaming with unearned confidence. "I've cornered the market on California minerals!"

I looked at his buckets. Gravel. Pure, worthless, sedimentary gravel.

"Moony, those are rocks."

"They're a *diversified mineral portfolio*," he corrected me. "Granite, quartz, some very promising limestone. I'm holding for the long term."

This was, I realised, value investing taken to its most absurd conclusion. Moony had found the one asset class that literally could not appreciate in value and declared it undervalued.

"The real prospectors," I told him, "follow the *volume*. See that creek bend? Yesterday, six miners pulled three ounces from that spot. That's a volume spike on a breakout level. That's where the gold is."

Moony shook his grey head. "Too volatile. I'm sticking with my rocks. They have strong fundamentals."

## Chapter 3: The Breakout at Sutter's Creek

I spent the next three days working the creek with my system. Opening range: first light to sunrise — map the geological formations. Then I'd wait for breakout: a flash of colour in the pan, confirmed by volume (three consecutive scoops with above-average particulate). Stop loss: if five pans came up empty, move to the next spot. No emotional attachment.

Day one: 1.2 ounces. Day two: 0.8 ounces. Day three: the breakout of all breakouts.

I hit a pocket seam. Gold sitting in a quartz vein like it was waiting for me to take the trade. I pulled four ounces in an hour. At $20.67 per ounce — the 1849 fixed price — that was $82.68 in a single session. The other miners gathered around, mouths open, watching the greatest opening range breakout in California mining history.

Moony waddled over, still clutching a handful of limestone. "That's very nice," he said, eyeing my gold. "But it's overextended. I'd wait for a pullback before mining more."

"Moony, gold doesn't *pull back*. It's gold. It's been trending for six thousand years."

"Exactly," he said. "Due for a correction."

## Chapter 4: Moony Gets Panned

The final humiliation came on Sunday. A trader from San Francisco rode into camp offering $30 an ounce — a 45% premium to the government price. I sold my week's haul for $248. The other miners cheered. Someone played a fiddle.

Moony, sensing opportunity at last, rushed forward with his seventeen buckets. "I'll sell too!" he announced. "Twenty dollars per bucket! Firm!"

The trader stared at the gravel. Then at Moony. Then back at the gravel.

"Sir, that's dirt."

"It's a diversified mineral portfolio with strong—"

"It's dirt."

Moony was last seen floating down the American River on an improvised raft made of overturned buckets, shouting about how the gravel market was cyclical and he was just early. The current carried him toward the Pacific, which felt appropriate — there's nothing like an ocean of liquidity to drown a bad thesis.

As for me, I took my $248 and bought a claim on the next creek over. The opening range looked promising, and the volume was building. In California, as in the markets, the gold goes to those who follow the signal — not those who sit on a pile of rocks and call it a strategy.

Somewhere out in the Pacific, Moony is probably still floating, telling the fish about his diversified mineral portfolio.

The fish are not impressed.
