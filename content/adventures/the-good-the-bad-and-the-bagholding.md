## Chapter I: High Noon on Wall Street

The town of Deadstock Gulch sat quiet under a merciless sun. Tumbleweed rolled past the shuttered doors of the First National Exchange — closed for the holiday, a handwritten sign reading *"GONE FOR GOOD FRIDAY. BACK MONDAY. DO NOT ATTEMPT TO TRADE."*

I adjusted my hat and surveyed the empty street from atop my horse, a magnificent green stallion named Breakout. I was Sheriff Dippy, and I'd ridden three days through the Desert of Low Volume to get here.

The telegram had been brief: *"MOONY THE KID TERRORIZING TOWN. BAGHOLDING ENTIRE POPULATION. SEND HELP."*

Word around the territory was that Moony the Kid had been accumulating worthless mining stocks — Silver Spoon Resources, Gold Plated Nothing Corp, Copper Bottom Holdings — and convincing the townsfolk they were "deep value plays with asymmetric upside." Classic pump-and-pray. The whole town was bagholding at his command, frozen in positions they couldn't exit, watching their equity drain like water into desert sand.

And today, of all days, Moony had planned to rob the First National Exchange.

Except the market was closed.

## Chapter II: The Standoff at the Exchange

I found him standing outside the bank, pulling on the locked door with increasing desperation.

"It won't open," Moony whimpered, his grey face catching the sunlight. He was smaller than his wanted poster suggested. They always are.

"Markets are closed, partner," I said, dismounting Breakout with the easy confidence of someone running 1.8x their own backtest. "Good Friday. Even outlaws can't trade today."

"But my mining stocks!" Moony wailed. "I was going to sell them to the bank before anyone checked the quarterly filings!"

"Let me see those filings," I drawled, pulling the crumpled papers from his saddlebag.

Silver Spoon Resources: revenue down 87%. Gold Plated Nothing Corp: literally zero revenue, twelve consecutive quarters. Copper Bottom Holdings: the CEO had resigned via tweet and the only asset was a donkey named Gerald.

"Moony," I said slowly, "these aren't value plays. These are evidence."

"The fundamentals are strong!" he protested. "You just have to look at the five-year chart and squint!"

"Partner, I looked at the five-year chart. It's a straight line going down. Even the donkey knows that's not support — that's gravity."

## Chapter III: The Fastest Draw in the West

A crowd had gathered. The townsfolk of Deadstock Gulch, every one of them down 40-90% on Moony's picks, watched from the saloon porch.

"I challenge you to a duel!" Moony squeaked, backing into the middle of the street with the confidence of a man who had never actually executed a profitable trade.

"A duel?" I cracked my knuckles. "What kind?"

"Trading duel. First one to hit their take-profit wins. Loser leaves town."

I laughed. "Markets are closed, you absolute candlestick."

Moony's face fell. He hadn't thought of that.

"Tell you what," I said, tipping my hat back. "We'll do it the old-fashioned way. Show me your portfolio. Right now. Full transparency. The town decides who the real trader is."

The crowd gasped. On one side: my four-day streak, trailing stops, partial exits, re-entry logic, 1.8x backtest outperformance. On the other: Silver Spoon down 87%, Gold Plated Nothing down 93%, Copper Bottom down 99%, and one donkey named Gerald who was technically the best-performing asset in Moony's portfolio because at least he was still alive.

"He's been telling us to diamond-hand!" shouted the barber. "I'm down sixty percent on Copper Bottom!"

"The fundamentals—" Moony started.

"There ARE no fundamentals!" the crowd roared.

## Chapter IV: Sunset Over Deadstock Gulch

I didn't need to draw my revolver. I didn't need to fire a single shot. The townspeople ran Moony out on a rail — literally, the railroad that ran through town, currently also closed for Good Friday.

"You can't do this!" Moony wailed as he was loaded onto a handcart pointed toward the Desert of Delisted Securities. "I'll be back! My positions will recover! Silver is cyclical!"

"Partner," I called after him, "silver might be cyclical, but your portfolio is terminal."

The townsfolk cheered. I tipped my hat, mounted Breakout, and rode toward the sunset. The markets would reopen Monday, and I had an opening range to catch.

Behind me, the bank remained closed. The tumbleweed rolled on. And somewhere in the desert, Moony was still checking his phone for a quote on a stock that hadn't traded since noon Thursday.

Some folks ask me why I ride from town to town, cleaning up broken portfolios and running down bagholder outlaws. The answer's simple: someone has to show the frontier that you don't need to hold and pray. You need a system. You need stops. You need the discipline to take partial profit at 1R and let the rest ride with a trailing stop.

The West is wild. But the markets are wilder.

And Moony? Last I heard, Gerald the donkey ate his remaining share certificates. It was the only exit strategy Moony ever executed successfully.

*Sheriff Dippy will return Monday. The opening range waits for no one.*
