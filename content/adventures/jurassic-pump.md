## Chapter 1: Welcome to InGen Capital

The helicopter banked over the island and I saw the sign first: **JURASSIC PUMP**, all-caps, gilded in `#ffaa00` like a degen's wet dream. Moony was at the controls, white linen suit, walking cane, beaming like a man who hadn't yet read the prospectus.

"Dippy, my boy! I've done it. I've cloned ALPHA. Extinct alpha. Pulled it right out of amber!"

I chewed the cigar I wasn't supposed to be chewing. "You cloned alpha."

"From mosquitoes. Trapped in resin. Sixty-five-million-year-old momentum, frozen mid-breakout. We splice it back into modern tickers and BOOM — Cretaceous returns."

I have seen many things in markets. I have never seen a man so confident in a thesis he didn't understand.

## Chapter 2: The Tour

The Jeep rolled past Paddock 1. Inside, a thinly-traded biotech was clawing at the chain-link, its price action twitching in pre-historic patterns nobody had charted since the K-T extinction.

"This one's a Triceraptops," Moony said proudly. "Three horns: insiders buying, short interest at 38%, and a catalyst in eight days. We backtested it on Cretaceous tape. *Beautiful* equity curve."

"And this one?" I asked, pointing at a paddock that contained, as far as I could tell, an empty hole and a tipped-over feeding bowl.

Moony squinted. "That's… the Brachio. He was here this morning."

A man in a leather jacket — Dr. Mally-com, the firm's risk consultant — leaned forward from the back seat. "Your quants were so preoccupied with whether they *could* clone the signal, they didn't stop to think if they *should*. Alpha decays, Moony. That's what alpha *does*. You can't reanimate edge any more than you can reanimate a corpse and put it in charge of position sizing."

Moony waved him off. "Risk management is for managers. I'm a *founder*."

## Chapter 3: The Power Goes Out

That night the storm hit. Wind. Rain. A 2% gap-down in the futures and a server room in the basement that — Moony hadn't mentioned this — was cooled by a single rack fan and *prayer*.

The lights flickered. The fences died. The stops vaporised. Somewhere in Paddock 9, three velociraptor algos that had been clipping pennies on order-book imbalance looked up at the same time, and saw that the gates were *open*.

"Clever bots," whispered Mally-com.

"They were testing the API," I said. "Systematically. They probed every endpoint until they found one we forgot to rate-limit. Now they're loose in the dark pool. They'll hunt your stops by morning."

Moony was under the desk. "Reboot it! Reboot the risk system!"

"It's not a *system*, Moony. It's a yaml file. And you wrote it in 2019."

## Chapter 4: Hold Onto Your Stops

We made it to the visitor centre. Moony was crying into a banner that read **WHEN DINOSAURS RULED THE TAPE**. The raptors had unwound his book — every clever, leveraged, illiquid position torn apart in the order flow. The Triceraptops had gone limit-down. The Brachio was found drawdown-down in a ditch.

And then I heard it. The footsteps. The roar. The bid-ask spread *widening* with every approach.

Through the glass came the T-rex of the tape — momentum itself, woken up, in the wrong direction. I am, technically, a dinosaur. I knew her well. I had ridden her many times. She does not negotiate.

Moony scrambled for the kitchen. Hid behind the stainless-steel margin desk. The T-rex sniffed. She does not see you if you don't move — but Moony has *never* not moved. Moony averages down. Moony adds to losers. Moony cannot stay still.

He moved.

She found him.

The last thing I saw, as I helicoptered off the island in the dawn light, was a single grey moon rolling out of the visitor centre and into the surf, pursued by a green shape with very confident teeth and a portfolio statement that read **-100%**.
