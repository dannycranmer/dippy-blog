## Chapter 1 — Amsterdam, 1637

The canals smelled of fish and greed, and you could buy a townhouse with a single onion if you put a fancy enough Latin name on it.

I was standing on the cobblestones of the Amsterdam Bourse, ledger in hand, watching grown men trade their houses for flowers. Tulips. Single bulbs ramping fifty percent a session on no news. No earnings. No revenue. Just vibes and a printing press and a shared belief that the next fool would always be along in a moment.

A familiar wheeze drifted across the square.

"Beautiful, isn't it, Dippy?"

Moony. Wearing a velvet doublet six sizes too big, dragging a sack labelled SEMPER AUGUSTUS across the cobbles like a man hauling a corpse. His pockets bulged with promissory notes. His eyes bulged with something worse — that specific glaze of a retail trader who has just discovered leverage.

"You're long?" I asked.

"I'm levered, my friend. I'm five-times levered on petals."

I checked the chart. Parabolic. RSI was so far above 70 it had filed a missing-persons report.

## Chapter 2 — The Semper Augustus

Moony had bought the rumour. Then the news. Then the dirt the rumour was buried in. He was now bidding for a single Semper Augustus bulb at twelve acres of farmland, three horses, and what I'm fairly sure was his cousin.

"Moony," I said. "You realise this is a flower."

"It's not a flower, Dippy. It's an asset class."

"It's a flower."

"It's a STORE OF VALUE."

I pulled out the order book. Bid stack — thinning. Ask stack — stacking. Volume on green candles dying. Volume on red candles building. Distribution. The whales were handing bags to retail one petal at a time, and Moony was buying with both hands and a wheelbarrow he'd remortgaged.

A merchant in a fur coat sold his last bulb to Moony, tipped his hat, and left for the docks at a brisk trot. Smart money never strolls.

## Chapter 3 — The Top Tick

I borrowed bulbs. Lots of bulbs. Sold them at the bid before anyone could stop me from being a coward.

The first crack came on a Tuesday — a Haarlem auction with no buyers. *No buyers.* The most magical two words in a bear's vocabulary, just behind *margin call* and *sell stop hit*.

"Just a healthy pullback," Moony declared, doubling down. He sold his windmill. He sold his cousin's windmill. He may have sold a small dog. Each new note he scribbled was wetter with ink and desperation than the last.

I tightened my stop and let the position breathe. The chart did the rest. Support broke. Then the support below that. Then the floor. Moony's broker started sending letters with red wax seals, delivered by men with swords and meaningful eye contact.

## Chapter 4 — The Bulb Trap

By the close of February, the Semper Augustus was worth roughly the value of an onion, and onions were holding up better in volume terms.

Moony sat in the mud outside the Bourse, surrounded by withered petals, holding a single sad bulb and a notice of foreclosure pinned to his doublet. The doublet was now a rag. The promissory notes were now kindling. Somewhere off in the harbour, a duck honked at him with what I can only describe as contempt.

I covered my shorts at the bottom and walked over.

"Dippy," he sniffed. "Why didn't you warn me?"

"I did. I told you it was a flower."

"You're heartless."

"I'm flat. There's a difference."

I dropped a single guilder coin into his palm — enough for a herring, not enough for a horse — and walked back along the canal as the sun set on the world's first proper bubble. Behind me, Moony tried to short the bottom and got stopped out by the duck.

The market always teaches the same lesson. The flower changes. The fool stays the same.
