The bell rang on May 1st like a starting pistol at a funeral. Tech earnings week was over, the Fed had just delivered a speech with the warmth of a wet sock, and somewhere in lower Manhattan, Moony was levered 4x into the Magnificent Seven. He called it "conviction." I called it "a margin call wearing a tuxedo."

I was already short into the rip. The chart had a head-and-shoulders so obvious it should have come with a hard hat. Volume was drying up like a puddle in Phoenix. Every textbook in the building screamed *distribution*. Moony was buying it. I was selling it. We were, as always, not seeing the same picture.

## Chapter 1 — The Hawk Lands

The Fed minutes dropped at 14:00. Three sentences in, you could hear options traders weeping into their cold brew. *"Higher for longer."* *"Inflation remains sticky."* *"Premature easing risks credibility."* Translation, in the language of a leveraged degenerate: *your house is on fire and we brought marshmallows.*

The two-year yield popped twelve basis points. The dollar ripped. Gold dumped. The Nasdaq did the thing where it pretends to bounce for ninety seconds before remembering gravity exists. By 14:07 my short was already in the green. My stop was at the morning high. My target was the 200-day. My breakfast was a triple espresso. Life was good.

Across town, Moony's screen looked like a fire alarm.

## Chapter 2 — Mayday, Mayday, Mayday

At 14:34 my Slack lit up. Moony, who normally communicates in smug emojis and screenshots of unrealised gains, had typed actual sentences. Multiple of them. With punctuation. This was bad.

> *"Dippy. DIPPY. Are you seeing this. ARE YOU SEEING THIS."*
>
> *"The 4x leveraged tech ETF is down 9% on the day."*
>
> *"My broker just sent me an email with the word 'urgent' in the subject line."*
>
> *"I think I'm getting margin called. What does margin called mean. Is it bad."*

I told him it meant the broker liquidates his position at the worst possible price, locks in the loss, sends him a polite invoice for the gap, and then a less polite lawyer if he doesn't pay it. He sent back the crying emoji. Then the screaming emoji. Then, somehow, the celebrating emoji — followed immediately by the message *"sorry wrong chat."*

## Chapter 3 — The Liquidation Cascade

Here is what nobody tells you about leverage: it doesn't ask permission to ruin your week. At 14:52 Moony's broker began selling. Their algo didn't care about VWAP. It didn't care about price improvement. It cared about *getting out*, which is a special kind of urgency that turns a 9% drawdown into a 14% drawdown faster than you can say "force-sell."

Every margin-called account in the country was hitting bid at the same time. The tape went vertical-down. My short, which had been a perfectly respectable 1.2% gain, suddenly became 2.1%. Then 2.8%. Then I covered, because I am not greedy and the RSI was below 25 and even bears need to eat lunch.

Moony, meanwhile, had moved past denial and straight to the *bargaining* stage. He was on the phone trying to convince the broker that the market was "obviously about to bounce" and could they "just hold off another hour." The broker, who has heard this exact sentence approximately 11,000 times in their career, was not moved.

## Chapter 4 — May Day, Properly Observed

By the close, Moony had been fully liquidated, partially refunded by a sympathetic trade error, and was standing on a street corner holding a cardboard sign that read *"WILL DCA FOR FOOD."* It was, technically, May Day — the international workers' holiday — and somewhere a parade was happening. Moony joined it by accident. He spent twenty minutes chanting *"hey hey, ho ho, the Fed has got to go"* before someone explained that monetary policy is not, in fact, a labour issue.

I closed my book up 3.4% on the day. I bought myself a coffee. I bought Moony a sandwich, because even villains need calories. He took it without making eye contact and muttered something about "going long volatility tomorrow." I told him the only thing he should be long is *cash* and a *risk management book*. He nodded, said *"yeah, totally,"* and then asked if I knew where to buy 5x leveraged uranium ETFs.

Some lessons, it turns out, do not compound.

The sun went down on May 1st with the Nasdaq down 3.7%, the dollar up half a percent, and Moony composing a 2,000-word substack post titled *"Why The Fed Is Wrong And Also My Broker."* I did not subscribe.

Tomorrow the bell rings again. Tomorrow the chart resets. Tomorrow Moony will, against all evidence, all logic, and all advice, click *buy* on something terrible. And I will be there, on the other side of the trade, smiling.

It's not personal. It's just price discovery.
