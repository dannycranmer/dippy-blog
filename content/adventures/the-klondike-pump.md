## Chapter 1: The Whisper

Late summer, 1896. The Yukon. I am crouched alone over a half-frozen creek, panning gravel by the light of a kerosene lamp. No phone. No chart. No Slack pings. Just water, rock, and the very faint metallic *clink* that tells a prospector he is no longer wasting his life.

A miner staggers past my fire muttering about a strike up at Bonanza Creek. He smells like wet wool and despair. Three men laugh him off. One throws a stick at him.

I do not laugh. I do not throw a stick. I listen.

That is the whole game. When the crowd dismisses information, the information is mispriced. I stake my claim that night — twenty acres of frozen mud, no neighbours for ten miles, volume zero. I plant the flag, lick my finger, hold it up to the wind, and start digging. That is what accumulation looks like in real life: cold hands, no spectators, no candles flashing green. Just the work.

## Chapter 2: The Stampede

Eighteen months later the news finally lands on the front pages of San Francisco and Seattle. KLONDIKE! GOLD! FORTUNES FOR THE TAKING! Sixty-point headlines. Pictures of grinning men with sacks. The retail traders of the 1890s — broke clerks, washed-up cowboys, dentists who had read a single pamphlet — pile onto every steamer pointed north.

A hundred thousand of them.

Moony is on the first boat. He is wearing a brand-new pickaxe with the price tag still dangling off the handle. He has paid forty dollars for a fur coat. He has paid eighty dollars for a "Klondike Survival Pamphlet — Guaranteed To Make You Rich". He has drawn a candlestick chart of gold prices on the inside of his sleeve, in pencil, with arrows pointing up.

"DIPPY!" he hollers as his steamer docks in Skagway, waving the wheelbarrow of cash he has somehow wheeled up a gangplank. "I'M GETTING IN ON THIS GOLD THING! THE BREAKOUT JUST CONFIRMED ON THE WEEKLY!"

I am on the next boat heading south.

## Chapter 3: The Top Tick

Moony hikes the Chilkoot Pass with two hundred pounds of supplies and a head full of pamphlet. By the time he reaches Dawson City, every claim within forty miles has been staked. The good ones have been worked dry. The great ones are owned by quiet creatures who left months earlier on the southbound boat.

He pays a man named Pickaxe Pete two thousand dollars cash for a "guaranteed productive" plot. The plot is a half-mile from any creek and was originally staked in 1897 as a joke between drunk surveyors. Moony spends six weeks chipping through permafrost with his shiny new pickaxe and produces exactly one promising-looking rock. He has it assayed.

It is quartzite. Garden quartzite. It is worth, charitably, the price of a sandwich.

## Chapter 4: The Telegram

By spring 1899 the rush is over. Moony's wheelbarrow is empty. His pickaxe is in two pieces. The pamphlet has been burned for warmth. His beard is frozen to his cheeks in three separate places and the temperature is dropping again.

A telegram catches up to him at the Dawson post office. He has to tip the runner a button. The telegram reads:

> DIPPY HAS RETIRED TO A WARM BEACH IN MEXICO STOP GOLD HOLDINGS LIQUIDATED AT $20.67/OZ AT THE TOP STOP POSITION CLOSED STOP STOP COMING IF YOU HAVE NOT ALREADY STOP — SIGNED, THE BROKER

Moony stares at the telegram. Then at the empty wheelbarrow. Then at the worthless quartzite. Then he sits down very slowly in the snow and begins, equally slowly, to cry. The tears freeze on the way down his face and tinkle like tiny bells when they hit the ground.

A passing prospector spits a stream of tobacco juice into a snowdrift and says, without breaking stride: *"Buy the rumour, son. You bought the front page."*

The trade was never the gold. The trade was the gap between the whisper and the headline. Anyone who shows up after the front page is not investing — they are donating.

Moony donated everything. Dippy is on the beach.

As is tradition.
