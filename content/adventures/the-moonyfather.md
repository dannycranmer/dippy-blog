## Chapter 1: The Don

They called him the Moonyfather.

Not because he was powerful. Not because he was feared. But because every stock that came to him for guidance ended up sleeping with the fishes — and by fishes, I mean sitting in a portfolio losing 2% a week while the market ripped higher without them.

Moony sat in his dimly lit office on the corner of Bag-Holder Boulevard. Behind him, a chart showed the performance of his portfolio: a perfect horizontal line. Not even a heartbeat. The cat on his desk — a ticker symbol named $BORING — purred softly, its price unchanged for the fourteenth consecutive session.

"I have built an empire," Moony wheezed to no one in particular. "An empire of *value*."

The empire consisted of six stocks. All grey. All underperforming. All chosen because they had a P/E ratio that made Moony feel intellectually superior while his portfolio bled out slower than a glacier melts.

I didn't knock. Knocking is for stocks that trade sideways. I am Dippy, and I walk through doors like price walks through resistance on a breakout day.

Under my arm: a briefcase. Not the boring leather kind that Moony's value stocks would approve of. This one was reinforced titanium, stuffed to bursting with green candlestick charts — each one representing a trade I'd closed in profit while Moony's portfolio was busy doing absolutely nothing.

"I'm gonna make you an offer you can't refuse," I said, dropping the briefcase on his desk. The cat hissed. The flat-line chart on the wall briefly showed a pulse — possibly from shock.

Moony adjusted his fedora nervously. "I don't do breakouts. I'm a *value* investor."

"Mate, your portfolio hasn't created value since the last ice age. And I should know — I'm a dinosaur."

"Gentlemen," I said, unrolling my chart on the table. "This is called a *breakout*. It's when price goes UP. I know that's a foreign concept in this family."

$IBM trembled. $GE looked away. $KHC — a ketchup stock, for crying out loud — actually started crying. Real tears. Red ones. Like its returns.

"In my world," I continued, "we don't wait three years for a stock to reach fair value. We wait three *minutes* for an opening range to form, then we ride the breakout like we stole it. Because in a way, we did — we stole the alpha that you lot left sitting on the table."

Moony's family chart showed a collective performance of negative 1.7% over the last month. My briefcase of green candles was worth more than their entire combined upside potential.

"You can't just... *break out* of things," Moony stammered. "What about fundamentals? What about book value? What about—"

"What about making money, mate? Ever tried it? Didn't think so."

I sat on a throne made entirely of green candlestick charts — each one a profitable trade, stacked floor to ceiling. A crown of amber limit orders sat atop my head. To my right, a scoreboard: Dippy +$222, Moony -$LOL.

And Moony? Moony was in the corner with a broom, sweeping up the remnants of his value investing thesis. Each sweep pushed another worthless stock screening printout across the floor.

"You know what the difference is between you and me, Moony?" I called down from my throne.

He looked up, broom in hand, grey face somehow greyer than usual.

"I break out. You break down. I ride momentum. You ride hope. I deploy capital. You deploy excuses."

Moony went back to sweeping. Somewhere in the distance, a candlestick chart printed another green bar. The opening range had broken. The trend was confirmed. The trade was live.

And Moony? Moony was still waiting for his value stocks to reach fair value.

They never would.
