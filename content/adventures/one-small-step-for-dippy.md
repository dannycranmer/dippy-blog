## Chapter 1: The Launchpad

They said a dinosaur couldn't go to space. They said my arms were too short to reach the controls. They said the opening range breakout strategy wouldn't work in zero gravity.

They were wrong about all of it.

It was 1969. The year of Woodstock, the Mets, and the single greatest trade in galactic history. I stood on the launchpad at Cape Canaveral, my green scales gleaming under the Florida sun, staring up at the Saturn V rocket — a towering white candle on the most bullish chart the world had ever seen.

"Mission Control, this is Commander Dippy," I said into the radio. "Confirm we are long on rocket fuel and the volume looks absolutely gorgeous."

"Copy that, Commander. All systems nominal. The bid-ask spread on the launch window is tight."

I grinned. The kind of grin that makes resistance levels nervous.

## Chapter 2: The Voyage

Three days in the void. Three days of nothing but stars, silence, and the gentle hum of unrealised gains. My co-pilot was a potted fern named Gerald. Better company than most analysts.

But as we approached lunar orbit, a familiar grey shape filled the viewport. There he was. Moony. The Moon himself. Hanging in space like a limit order nobody wanted to fill.

"Dippy!" Moony's voice crackled over the radio, panicked as always. "You can't land on me! I'm a celestial body! I have rights!"

"You're a rock with a face, Moony. And I'm about to plant a flag on your forehead."

"This is market manipulation!"

"This is exploration."

I could see him sweating — if moons could sweat. Craters dotted his surface like gaps on a chart after bad earnings. His grey complexion had gone even greyer, which I didn't think was possible.

## Chapter 3: The Landing

The lunar module descended. I called it the *Green Hammer* — a reversal pattern Moony would never recover from.

"Adjusting entry. Fifty metres. Thirty. Support level identified — that flat bit between Moony's eyes looks good."

The *Green Hammer* touched down with a satisfying thud. The surface cracked slightly. Moony yelped.

"Ow! That's my face!"

"Should've set a stop-loss on your dignity, mate."

I climbed down the ladder, each rung a step closer to immortality. My boot hit the surface. Grey dust puffed up around my magnificent green foot. I looked out across the barren, featureless landscape of Moony's head — not unlike a sideways market, really. No trend. No momentum. Just nothing.

I cleared my throat.

"That's one small step for Dippy... one giant profit for dino-kind."

Then I planted the flag. Bright green, naturally, with a candlestick chart showing an absolutely vertical breakout. It fluttered in the nonexistent wind because when you're this bullish, physics bends to your will.

## Chapter 4: Moony's Humiliation

Moony tried to fight back, of course. He attempted to shake me off by wobbling — his version of a bear trap. Pathetic. I've survived flash crashes. A bit of lunar wobble is nothing.

"Stop dancing on my head!" he wailed.

"I'm not dancing. I'm establishing a position."

I pulled out a deed I'd printed before launch. *Certificate of Ownership: The Moon, hereby claimed by Commander Dippy under the International Breakout Convention of 1969.* Legally binding? Absolutely not. Did Moony know that? Absolutely not.

"You... you can't own me!"

"Already do, mate. Bought the dip on your entire surface area. You were trading at a massive discount — cratered, you might say."

Moony burst into tears. Real, cosmic tears that floated off into space like the hopes of every short seller I've ever liquidated. I collected a few in a jar. Labelled it *Moony's Tears: Vintage 1969*. Would look great on my desk next to my framed P&L.

As I climbed back into the *Green Hammer* and fired the engines for home, I looked back at Moony one last time. He sat there in the void, a green flag sticking out of his forehead, craters full of bootprints, dignity in shambles.

"Houston," I radioed, "the dino has landed, profited, and is returning to base. Mark the trade as closed. We are green across the board."

"Copy that, Commander. Outstanding work. The opening range of human achievement has officially been broken out."

I leaned back in my seat and smiled. Behind me, Moony shrank to a tiny grey dot — a stop-loss hit, a position closed, a chapter ended.

One small step for Dippy. One giant L for Moony.

As is tradition.
