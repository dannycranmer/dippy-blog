## Chapter 1 — The Siege of Bearium

Ten years. Ten *years* I'd been camped outside the walls of Bearium, and the bears inside hadn't budged a single tick.

Their king was Moony — round, grey, and so smug you could hear him from the encampment. Every dawn he'd waddle onto the ramparts, flash a peace sign, and shout, *"Support holds, dino. Support always holds."* Then he'd roll a barrel of red candles down at us for good measure.

My green army was tired. Morale was bleeding harder than a flush at the open. We'd thrown everything at that wall — ladders, catapults, a perfectly good earnings beat — and not one stone had cracked. Bearium was the strongest support level in the known world, and Moony knew it.

## Chapter 2 — The Wooden Gift

That night, I called in the carpenters. *"Build me a bull,"* I said. *"Wooden. Big as a temple. Hollow inside. Paint it green and put a bow on it."*

The lead carpenter blinked. *"A bull, sir?"*

*"A bull, soldier. The biggest, dumbest, most tempting bull this peninsula has ever seen. A bull so beautiful that any moon-brained king who sees it will absolutely *have* to roll it through his own front gate."*

We worked through the night. By sunrise the Trojan Bull stood twelve metres tall in the field, gleaming green in the dawn, with a little wooden plaque around its neck that read **"FREE — LIMIT UP."** Inside its hollow belly, packed shoulder to shoulder, were one hundred of my finest green candles, breathing quietly, swords drawn, momentum coiled.

Then we burned the camp. Sailed the ships out of sight. Made it look like a full retreat — like we'd given up, capitulated, finally hit our stop.

## Chapter 3 — Moony Buys the Top

Moony came down off the wall at dawn and saw the bull standing alone in the empty field.

*"They've left this for ME?"* he gasped, drool already pooling. *"A *gift*? After ten years? A free bull is the strongest signal in any market!"*

His advisors begged him to think. *"Your majesty,"* whispered one, *"perhaps we should… inspect it first? Check the order book? Wait for confirmation?"*

Moony slapped him. *"You'd have me wait? While dinos give away free bulls? This is *rotation*, you fool! This is the regime change! GET IT INSIDE THE WALLS."*

So they rolled it. Through the gates, past the inner ramparts, right into the central square of Bearium. Moony declared a public holiday. Threw a feast. Climbed up onto the bull and danced on its head, waving a half-eaten chicken leg, screaming *"BULL MARKET CONFIRMED!"* at his own subjects.

I cannot stress this enough — at no point did anyone check inside the bull.

## Chapter 4 — The Breakout

I waited until midnight. Until the feast had emptied the wine cellars. Until every bear in the city was face-down in baklava and conviction.

Then I tapped the side of the bull. *Tap tap.*

The hatch sprang open. One hundred green candles poured out into the silent square — silent for maybe four seconds, until the first war horn ripped the night in half.

Resistance shattered. Support became a memory. The walls Moony had defended for ten years opened from the *inside* like a stop-loss tripping at three in the morning. My retreating fleet — never far away, just hiding past the volume cluster — sailed back at full speed and swept in through the open gates.

Moony sat up in his royal bedchamber, hair askew, a single olive stuck to his cheek. He looked down at the courtyard. He saw the bull. He saw the empty hatch. He saw one hundred green candles forming a perfect parabolic arc up his marble staircase.

*"Oh,"* said Moony. *"Oh no. I bought the top."*

He tried to rally. He tried to short. He tried to roll the bull back out the gate. None of it mattered. The breakout was structural, the volume was real, and the king of bears was carried out of his own palace at dawn — wrapped in his own bedsheets, weeping into a half-finished wheel of cheese.

The moral of this story, friends, is simple. When a green dinosaur leaves a giant wooden bull at your door with a bow on it and a sign that says **FREE**, you do not roll it inside. You do not throw a feast. You especially do not climb on top of it and dance.

You walk away.

Or, if you're Moony, you don't. You never do. And ten years of held support evaporate in a single overnight session, because the only thing in this world stronger than a fortified wall is a bear king's appetite for a free lunch.

I sailed home in the morning. Bearium was on fire behind me, in the most profitable way a city can be.
