History remembers December 17, 1903, as the day humanity first slipped the surly bonds of earth. What history forgets is that there were *two* prototypes on that beach. Mine flew. The other one — well. We'll get to the other one.

## Chapter 1: The Bicycle Shop

Dayton, Ohio. 1902. I'm hunched over a workbench in a bicycle repair shop that smells like grease and ambition. My ledger has two columns: **lift** and **drag**. Lift is good. Drag is what kills you. The whole game is maximising the first while shrinking the second — and if that doesn't sound like a trading thesis, you haven't been paying attention.

The bell on the door jingles. In waddles a grey, spherical, slightly damp-looking moon in a tweed jacket.

"Dippy," says Moony, "I have come to inform you that your flying machine concept is *fundamentally* unworkable. The smartest minds of the century — Lord Kelvin, Simon Newcomb — they've all said heavier-than-air flight is impossible. I myself have a patent pending on a competing design. It is superior in every way."

I look up. "What's it made of, Moony?"

"Cheese."

"And what propels it?"

"Moonbeams."

I write *short* in my ledger and underline it twice.

## Chapter 2: The Dunes

December 17, 1903. Kill Devil Hills, North Carolina. The wind is howling out of the north at twenty-seven miles per hour. The sand is in my teeth. The Atlantic is grey and bored, like Moony but wetter.

The Wright Flyer sits on its launch rail — 605 pounds of spruce, muslin, and bicycle chain. Across the dunes, Moony has erected his own contraption: a cheese wheel with two flaps glued to it and a string. He has a press photographer.

I do my pre-flight check. Wing warp: responsive. Engine: twelve horsepower, purring. Wind: dead into my face, which means **lift**. I climb aboard, lay flat, and grip the controls.

The setup is perfect. Wide base. Tight consolidation. Volume building. All I need is the breakout.

Off in the distance, Moony shouts: "I shall be the first to FLY! Witness, photographer! WITNESS!" He gives the cheese wheel a hopeful shove. It rolls four feet down the dune and stops in a tide pool.

I release the restraint wire. The Flyer surges forward. Twelve seconds of throttle. The skids leave the rail. The skids leave the *earth*.

I am flying.

## Chapter 3: The Press Photo

I land softly. The skids carve two clean lines in the sand. I climb out, dust myself off, and walk over to where my brother Orville (also me, in this telling, because I contain multitudes) is doing a small dignified victory dance.

Moony is now waist-deep in the tide pool, trying to push the cheese wheel back uphill. The press photographer, sensing where the real story is, swivels his camera ninety degrees and snaps a picture of *me* standing next to the Flyer with my chest puffed out.

That photograph runs in newspapers from San Francisco to Stockholm. The headline: **DINOSAUR CONQUERS SKY.** The subhead: **MOON THING SOAKED.**

Moony writes a letter to the editor demanding a correction. The editor publishes the letter. The correction reads: *"In our previous coverage, we failed to mention that Mr. Moony's cheese-based flying device was, in fact, an aged gouda. We regret the error."*

## Chapter 4: The Twelve-Second Trade

Here's the trading lesson, kids, since I know you came for one.

Twelve seconds. One hundred and twenty feet. That's all the first flight was. Looks like a scratch trade on a chart — barely a wick. But it was the *proof of concept*. It showed that lift could exceed drag. That a setup could become a breakout. That a contrarian bet on heavier-than-air, against every expert in the world, could pay off in twelve seconds and change every market for the next century.

Moony bet on the consensus. Moony bet on cheese. Moony got the tide pool.

I bet on the math. I bet on the tape. I bet on myself.

I got the sky.

Don't short the dino. Don't short the Flyer. And for the love of all that is holy, **don't fly cheese.**

The end.
