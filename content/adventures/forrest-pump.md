## Chapter 1: The Bench

Mama always said a portfolio was like a box of chocolates. You never know what you're gonna get — unless you have a stop-loss.

I was sittin' on a bench in Greenbow, Alabama, my green tail tucked under me, a candle chart foldin' out across my lap. Bus came. Bus went. A grey lump rolled up next to me and sighed like a margin call.

"That's a stupid lookin' dinosaur," he said.

"Ma'am, my name's Dippy. Dippy the Dino. Folks call me Forrest Pump 'cause I run trades to the moon."

The grey lump squinted. "I'm Moony. I'm short."

"That's mighty fine. I'm long."

I told Moony everythin'. About the time I bought the dip on a tropical storm warning. About the time I held a winner through a full earnings cycle 'cause Mama said winners run. About the time Sergeant Hannibal Bear tried to flush me out at the lows and I just… kept holdin'.

"You never trim," Moony said. "Eventually you'll get destroyed."

"Maybe so, Moony. Maybe so. But I got conviction. And conviction's like a peanut butter sandwich. Nutritious."

## Chapter 2: Run, Dippy, Run

Then one day, for no particular reason, I decided to run.

I ran a long position. I ran it through earnings. I ran it through an FOMC. I ran it through three CPI prints, two regional bank failures, and one sovereign downgrade. My beard grew long. My P&L grew longer. My trailing stop crept up behind me like a loyal dog.

Folks started followin' me. Twitter accounts. CNBC anchors. A guy in a sandwich board sayin' "REPENT, THE BUBBLE IS NIGH."

When I finally stopped, I turned around and said, "I'm pretty tired. I think I'll go home now." Closed the trade at +247%.

The crowd went quiet. They were waitin' for me to tell them what to do next. So I said the only thing Mama ever taught me about exits:

"That's all I have to say about that."

And I walked away with the gains.

## Chapter 3: Lieutenant Moony

Years later, I joined the army. Volatility was my drill sergeant. We got dropped into a market sell-off — I forget the name of the war. There was a lot of red.

My buddy Bubba dreamed of openin' a shrimp boat. He had a list of every shrimp dish you could make. Shrimp gumbo. Shrimp scampi. Shrimp leveraged ETF. He stepped on a moving average and didn't make it.

That left me with Lieutenant Moony. He'd lost his stops in the trade. Said he wanted to die in battle, like a real bear.

Hurricane season came. The market crashed seventy-five percent. Everyone closed their boats. Everyone except me. I was out there in the spread yellin', "IS THAT ALL YOU GOT, MR. MARGIN CALL?!"

When the storm cleared, every shrimp boat in the bay was on the bottom. Every short was buried. Lieutenant Moony was floatin' face-down in his own bid-ask spread.

I bought the wreckage at the bid. Bubba Pump Shrimp Co. Six hundred boats. Market cap of one billion dollars. Mama would've been proud.

## Chapter 4: Run, Moony, Run

Lieutenant Moony came back one last time, in a wheelchair he claimed was made of "bagholder feels." He'd shorted my company. The cover was eatin' him alive.

"You promised me," he wheezed. "You promised the bubble would pop."

"It did, Moony. It just popped on you."

I gave him a chocolate from my box of stocks. He looked at it real careful. It was a covered call.
