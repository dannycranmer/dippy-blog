## Chapter 1 — The S-1 From Hell

Moony walked into my office carrying a 400-page prospectus and a hoodie two sizes too big. "Dippy," he wheezed, "we're going public." I looked up from my order book. "You don't have revenue." He grinned. "We have *narrative*."

The ticker was **$MGPT**. The pitch: *MoonyGPT — an AI-powered autonomous trading agent that learns faster than the market.* The moat: a single Python script that imported the `random` library and renamed the output `alpha`. The valuation: forty-two billion dollars.

I flipped to the risk factors section. Page 87: *"Our product does not currently function."* Page 88: *"Our CEO is a moon."* Page 89 was just a picture of Moony shrugging.

"Bro," I said, "this is a zero." Moony nodded seriously. "That's why we're pricing at the top of the range."

## Chapter 2 — Ring the Bell

The roadshow was a blur of espresso, empty adjectives, and slide decks where every chart went up and to the right because Moony had drawn them in crayon. Allocators nodded. Buyside analysts scribbled. Somebody from a Miami family office wired him eighty million dollars while physically in the bathroom.

The book was ten times oversubscribed. Moony priced at $88. Opening print: $142. By 10:02 a.m. it was $210 and Moony was kissing the bell like it owed him money.

I watched the tape from across the floor. Volume was insane — the kind of parabolic blow-off that makes new traders think trees grow to the sky and old traders reach for the short ladder. The RSI was melting the screen. The chart didn't have candles anymore, just a single green vertical line labeled **"vibes."**

"Dippy, get in!" Moony screamed over the PA. "You're missing it! This is the *future*!"

I quietly loaded a short at $218, stop at $235. Textbook exhaustion. The only indicator I actually needed was the fact that Moony was happy.

## Chapter 3 — The Lock-Up That Wasn't

Here is the thing about IPOs, dear reader: insiders have a lock-up. They cannot sell for 180 days. This is a legal agreement, sworn in ink, backstopped by investment banks, enforced by the SEC, and understood by every CEO on earth.

Except Moony. Moony read the lock-up agreement, saw the word "agreement," assumed it was optional, and dumped his entire position at 10:47 a.m. on day one.

The filing hit the wire at 10:51. By 10:52 every algo on the street had tagged $MGPT with the neon skull emoji. By 10:54 the stock was $95. By 10:56 it was $31. By 10:58 the NYSE had halted it, un-halted it, re-halted it, and then a guy in a vest just unplugged the ticker.

## Chapter 4 — The Post-Mortem

I covered at $34. Clean four-figure print. Volume-weighted, risk-adjusted, emotionally satisfying.

Moony called me from what sounded like the back of an Uber. "Dippy," he sniffled, "the SEC wants to *talk* to me." I said, "Yes, Moony. That happens when you sell during a lock-up." He paused. "Is talking bad?"

The class-action was filed before the closing bell. The underwriters pulled their green shoe. CNBC ran a three-hour special titled *"The AI Bubble: We Told You So (We Did Not Tell You So)."* Moony's forty-two-billion-dollar baby closed its first day at a $900M market cap, most of which was the value of the office chairs.

The lesson, friend: narrative fills the book, but the tape tells the truth. Momentum is a loan from the future, and the future always calls it in. When the CEO is a moon, short the moon.

Moony is now CEO of a new company. It's called *MoonyGPT 2: The Comeback.* Same script. Same moon. Same ending.

**Post-trade review:** entered the short on euphoria, exited on fear, risk-managed the stop above the peak. The tape doesn't care about the narrative. It only cares about the order book. And the order book told me everything I needed to know the moment Moony kissed the bell.

Short the moon. Always short the moon.
