As far back as I can remember, I always wanted to be a trader. Moony just wanted to be made.

Chapter 1: As Far Back As I Can Remember

As far back as I can remember, I always wanted to be a trader. To me, being a trader was better than being President of the United States. Even before I first wandered into Paulie’s brokerage on the corner of 9th and Avenue D, I knew I wanted to be a part of them. To me, it meant being somebody in a neighbourhood that was full of HODLers. They were somebodies. They had ticker tape. They had risk officers. They had stop-losses they actually used.

And then there was Moony.

Paulie's brokerage. Green ticks on the wall. Moony already looking nervous in the corner.

Chapter 2: Funny How?

We were sitting at the back of the diner. Booth four. The one Paulie liked because the candles always closed green from that angle. I was telling a story about a long I’d held through the open — held it like a vise, watched it run twenty handles, dumped it into the morning auction. Clean. Surgical. The kind of trade you tell your kids about, if your kids cared, which they don’t.

Moony was laughing. Hard. Wheezing.

I said, “It was a good trade.”

Moony’s face went still. The whole booth went quiet.

“Profitable how?” he said. “Like, profitable how? I mean, what’s profitable about it?”

“You know,” I said. “It made money. The way you said it.”

“Wha— what way? Wh-what way did I say it? What way did I say it?”

I looked at him. He looked at me. Behind him, on the screen above the bar, his entire portfolio was bleeding out in real time. Down 4%. Down 6%. Down 9% on a single position he’d 5x’d into earnings against my advice.

“Moony,” I said quietly, “your account is down.”

He looked over his shoulder. Looked back at me. The whole booth started laughing again. It was the first time in his life Moony had been the punchline of his own joke and didn’t realise it until the candles closed.

The screen above the bar. Moony's portfolio in real time. The whole booth is laughing.

Chapter 3: The Lufthansa Heist

Then came the score. The big one. Paulie called it the Lufthansa, because every score worth doing has a name. We called it the open auction. Six million in liquidity dropping out of the imbalance window at 9:30:00 sharp. Coordinated. Surgical. Everyone had a job. I was on tape. Moony — and against my better judgment — Moony was on stops.

“All you have to do,” I told him, “is not move them.”

“I won’t move them.”

“Don’t move them.”

“I said I won’t move them, Dippy.”

He moved them.

Within four minutes of the open, Moony had widened every stop on the book by a full percent because the first candle “looked scary.” That’s a quote. That’s verbatim. It looked scary. Six million dollars of orchestrated edge, and Moony watched a single red wick and decided to renegotiate the entire premise of risk management with himself in the bathroom mirror.

By 9:45 we’d been stopped out of nothing — because there were no stops left. The trade ran twelve points. We caught one. Paulie didn’t speak to me for a week. Moony sent me a meme of a dog in a tie and said, “we ride again on Tuesday.”

There was no Tuesday.

The chart told the truth. The stops never got hit. Moony moved them anyway.

Chapter 4: The Suburbs

It’s the last day. I’ve been telling you this whole story from a kitchen counter in suburbia. The light is grey. The eggs are powdered. I’m cooking pasta with ketchup because I shorted my whole personality to get here, and I miss the diner, and I miss the booth, and I miss the open. But I don’t miss Moony.

Moony got made the way every Moony gets made. He oversized into a flat tape, levered into an FOMC, and tried to “trade his way out” of a 30% drawdown by averaging into the close. Last I heard, his broker was sending him certified letters. Last I heard, he was answering them. He’d been keeping a P&L journal in red sharpie on a napkin titled “Comeback Szn.” There was no comeback. There was only margin.

They picked him up at his apartment on a Tuesday morning. Not the cops. Worse. Risk. They took his platform, his data feeds, his Bloomberg, his charts. They left him with a Robinhood login and a paper account and told him to practice.

I think about Moony a lot. I think about the booth, and the candles, and the sound the open used to make. I think about how Moony always said he wanted to be made — to be somebody — and how he never understood that in this business, the only way to be made is to stay made. Cut your losers. Trail your winners. Sit when there’s no edge. Don’t move your stops because the candle “looks scary.”

I’m an average trader now. It’s the worst thing in the world.

But I’m still here.

And Moony? Moony is paper trading.

Suburbia. Grey light. Pasta with ketchup. And somewhere out there, Moony, paper trading.

That’s how it ends. Not with a bang. Not with a margin call. With a paper account, a quiet morning, and the slow, expensive understanding that the diner was never coming back.

And I’m still here. Booth four. Green candles only.

Stay made.
