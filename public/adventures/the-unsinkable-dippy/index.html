The Unsinkable Dippy

20 Mar 2026

When Moony's luxury cruise hits an iceberg of margin calls, only one dino keeps his portfolio afloat.

adventurehistoricalmoony

Chapter I: All Aboard the RMS Moonytanic

The year was 1912. The grandest vessel ever built sat gleaming in the Southampton docks — the RMS Moonytanic, a floating palace of leveraged positions and uncovered shorts. Captain Moony stood on the bridge, his grey face beaming with the confidence of a trader who’d never seen a red candle.

“She’s unsinkable,” Moony declared to the assembled press, adjusting his captain’s hat. “I’ve loaded this ship with nothing but momentum plays and zero stop losses. What could possibly go wrong?”

I watched from the first-class promenade deck, sipping a green smoothie. I hadn’t booked this cruise for the luxury. I’d booked it because when someone says “unsinkable,” the universe starts sharpening icebergs.

The RMS Moonytanic sets sail, loaded to the gunwales with leveraged positions and hubris.

Chapter II: Iceberg, Dead Ahead

Three days into the voyage, the ship’s lookout — a nervous quant named Frederick — spotted something on the horizon. Not an iceberg. Worse. A wall of selling pressure.

“Captain Moony! The market’s turning! I’m seeing massive volume on the sell side — we need to cut exposure!”

Moony squinted through his telescope. “Nonsense. That’s just a consolidation pattern. We’ll break through to new highs by morning. Full speed ahead.”

I’d already moved my capital to the lifeboats. Rule number one of trading: when the captain refuses to set stop losses, you quietly hedge your positions and locate the nearest exit.

The iceberg hit at 11:40 PM — a cascading margin call that ripped through every deck. Moony’s leveraged longs started unwinding faster than you could say “dead cat bounce.” The ship groaned. Positions crumbled. The orchestra on the poop deck switched from playing “Bull Market Serenade” to “Nearer My Stop Loss to Thee.”

The Iceberg of Hidden Risk — Moony only saw the top. The margin calls lurked beneath.

Chapter III: Women, Children, and Hedged Positions First

Chaos erupted. Passengers scrambled for the lifeboats, but Moony had replaced half of them with additional leverage capacity. Brilliant.

“Don’t panic!” Moony shouted from the tilting bridge. “This is just a healthy pullback! We’ll buy the dip!”

The ship was listing at forty-five degrees. His portfolio was listing at negative ninety.

I launched my lifeboat — a tidy little vessel I’d named the SS Risk Management. Inside were my hedged positions, my trailing stops, and a thermos of victory tea. As the Moonytanic’s stern rose into the air, I could see Moony still on the bridge, frantically trying to average down on a sinking ship.

“Moony!” I called across the freezing water. “You can’t buy the dip when the dip is the Atlantic Ocean!”

He didn’t listen. He never does.

The SS Risk Management sails calmly while Moony goes down with his positions.

Chapter IV: The Depths of Liquidation

The RMS Moonytanic slipped beneath the waves at 2:20 AM, taking 1,500 leveraged positions to the bottom of the Atlantic. Moony was last seen clinging to a floating candlestick chart, muttering about how “the fundamentals are still strong.”

A rescue ship — the RMS Breakout — picked me up at dawn. I’d made a tidy profit shorting Moonytanic stock on the way down. Don’t look at me like that. The market is the market.

They eventually fished Moony out of the water three days later, bloated with seawater and unrealised losses. His first words upon revival? “I just need one more margin extension.”

They found the wreck seventy-three years later, two miles down on the ocean floor. My short position was still in profit. Moony’s portfolio was still underwater.

Some things are truly unsinkable. My returns. Moony’s capacity for self-destruction. And the iron law that a dino with a stop loss will always outlast a moon without one.

“I’m the king of the ORB!” I shouted from the bow of the rescue ship, arms spread wide, green scales gleaming in the North Atlantic sunrise.

Moony gurgled something from a puddle on the deck. It might have been “help.” It might have been “hodl.” With Moony, it’s hard to tell the difference.
