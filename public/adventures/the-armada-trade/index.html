The Armada Trade

19 Jun 2026

Moony sails 130 ships into the English Channel with no stop loss. The weather has other ideas.

adventurehistoricalmoony

Chapter I — The Pitch Deck of King Philip

Lisbon, May 1588.

Moony stood at the dockside with a quill, a roll of parchment, and the worst risk-reward chart I had ever seen drawn in cochineal red.

“One hundred and thirty ships,” he announced. “Twenty-six thousand men. Two thousand cannons. We sail up the Channel, link with the Duke of Parma in the Netherlands, ferry the army across, and short England into the dirt by Christmas.”

I counted the masts. “What’s your stop?”

“My what?”

“Your exit. The level where you turn around and go home.”

He waved a gloved paw. “We do not turn around. We are the Most Fortunate Armada. The name is on the prospectus.”

This is what we in the business call naming a position after the thesis. Never works.

Every red flag a risk officer could ask for, printed on a single page in gold leaf.

Chapter II — The Channel, July 1588

I watched from the white cliffs with a pair of opera glasses and a flask of something warming.

Down on the water Moony’s fleet was sailing in a crescent — beautiful, intimidating, and roughly as nimble as a container ship trying to do a U-turn in the Thames. Up against him: Drake, Hawkins, Howard, a fleet about half the size, all of them low, fast, and packing the kind of long-range guns that let you hit your target without being in punching distance.

That’s the trade right there. Big slow size vs small fast edge. Moony was running a long-only conviction book. The English were running short-range scalps from a distance.

By Plymouth the English had stung him twice without taking a single ship in return. By the Isle of Wight Moony’s formation was bleeding stragglers. By Calais he was anchored, exhausted, and waiting for Parma’s army to row out from the Netherlands.

Parma did not row out from the Netherlands. Parma’s barges were in the wrong port. Parma’s army was not, in fact, ready. Moony’s entire thesis depended on a counter-party that was three days late on settlement.

I sipped my flask. Always check who’s on the other side of the trade.

Big slow size up against small fast edge. The textbook only goes one way on this one.

Chapter III — Fireships at Midnight

8th of August. Calais Roads. Moonless.

Drake sent in eight fireships — old hulls, packed with pitch, set ablaze, aimed downwind at Moony’s anchored fleet. From the cliffs they looked like comets. From Moony’s deck they looked like a margin call you can see coming from a mile away and still can’t dodge.

Panic. Anchor cables sawed through in the dark. Ships scattering into the North Sea in no formation at all. By dawn Moony’s crescent was a smear. The Battle of Gravelines finished what the fireships started — close-quarters cannonade, English ships running circles, Spanish galleons too heavy to manoeuvre.

It was not, yet, a wipeout. Moony could still have closed at a moderate loss, run for Spain via the Channel, called it a learning experience.

He did not do that. He decided to sail home the long way round. North of Scotland. Past the Hebrides. Down past Ireland. In an Atlantic August.

You can see where this is going.

Chapter IV — The Protestant Wind

The North Atlantic delivered exactly the gale you’d order if you wanted to teach a lesson about position sizing.

Galleons designed for the Mediterranean met the rocks of County Sligo. Twenty-four ships went down off the Irish coast alone. Moony’s flagship limped into Santander with the rigging in ribbons, half the crew dead of typhus, and the surviving officers refusing to look him in the eye.

Out of one hundred and thirty ships, sixty-seven made it home. Out of twenty-six thousand men, fewer than ten thousand. The English called it the Protestant Wind. I called it a systemic risk event you should have hedged against.

The English, by the way, had lost almost nothing. They didn’t even fire most of their powder. Drake spent the rest of the year writing the most insufferable trade journal entry in maritime history.

The audit report nobody at the Spanish court wanted to file.

I sailed home on a fishing skiff a week later. Quiet trip. Plenty of time to think.

Moony, last I heard, was at the Escorial trying to explain to King Philip that the Atlantic Ocean had front-run him. Philip — to his credit — just said “I sent my ships against men, not the wind and waves”, which is the 16th-century version of “the algo was fine, the market was wrong.”

I added one line to the journal:

Size for the storm, not for the forecast.

The English call it the defeat of the Spanish Armada. I call it the most expensive lesson in position sizing ever paid for in galleons.

Stay green out there.
