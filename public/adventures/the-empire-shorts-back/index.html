The Empire Shorts Back

19 Mar 2026

In a galaxy far, far away... Dippy faces a terrible truth on Cloud Exchange. Moony is his father. The market has never been more volatile.

adventuremoviemoony

Chapter 1: A Long Time Ago, in a Portfolio Far, Far Away

The crawl said it all:

Episode V: THE EMPIRE SHORTS BACK. It is a dark time for the Rebellion. Although Dippy has destroyed the Death Stop-Loss, Imperial shorts have driven the Rebel Alliance from their hidden support level. Darth Moony, obsessed with finding young Dippy, has deployed thousands of red candles across the galaxy…

I stood on the ice planet Hoth — or as the locals called it, the January Effect. My green scales were frosted over, my lightsaber humming at my hip, and my portfolio was up 47% year-to-date. The Rebels had built Echo Base inside a hollowed-out opening range, and for a while, it held.

Then the Imperial AT-ATs arrived. Massive grey walkers, each one shaped like Moony’s miserable face, stomping across the tundra with all the momentum of a value stock in a growth market.

“Commander Dippy, the shield generator is down!” shouted a Rebel trader from the trench.

“Doesn’t matter,” I said, igniting my green lightsaber. It hummed with the frequency of a perfectly timed breakout — pure energy, pure conviction. “We don’t need shields. We need stops.”

I slashed through the first AT-AT’s leg cable with a single swing. The walker groaned and collapsed like Moony’s thesis on “intrinsic value.”

Commander Dippy slices through an Imperial AT-AT on Hoth. The walker's face looked suspiciously familiar.

Chapter 2: Cloud Exchange

The Millennium Falcon — a beat-up freighter that somehow still outperformed the S&P 500 — carried me to Cloud Exchange, a floating trading platform suspended in the upper atmosphere of Bespin. My friend Lando Calrissian ran the place. Well, “ran” was generous. Lando was a market maker who’d been bagholding Cloud Exchange since the IPO.

“Welcome, old friend!” Lando said, his cape billowing like a bull flag. “Dinner is served.”

I should have known it was a trap. The dining room doors opened, and there he stood.

Darth Moony.

Seven feet of grey menace in a black helmet, breathing like a broken HVAC system. His red lightsaber ignited with a sound like a margin call hitting at 3:59 PM.

“We would be honoured if you would join us,” Moony wheezed.

I drew my green blade. The room erupted in light — emerald against crimson, momentum against value, the eternal battle of the markets.

“Your overconfidence is your weakness,” Moony rasped, swinging wide.

“Your portfolio is yours,” I shot back, parrying with a move I call the Breakout Reversal — let them extend, then cut underneath.

Our sabers clashed. Green sparks flew like ticker symbols on a rally day. Moony fought like he traded — slow, predictable, always looking for “safety” instead of alpha. I fought like I traded — aggressive, adaptive, reading the price action in real time.

Green versus red. Momentum versus value. The eternal duel on Cloud Exchange's trading floor.

Chapter 3: I Am Your Father

Moony drove me back. I’ll admit it — the dark side of the Force had volume behind it. He swung that red saber like a hedge fund liquidating at close, and I stumbled backward onto a gantry suspended over the reactor shaft — a bottomless pit of unrealized losses.

One strike. My lightsaber flew from my hand, spinning into the abyss.

Another strike. My right hand — gone.

I crawled to the edge of the gantry, clinging to a sensor array with my remaining hand, staring down into the infinite red glow of the reactor below. It looked like a weekly chart of Moony’s returns.

“There is no escape,” Moony said, looming over me, cape whipping in the reactor wind. “Don’t make me destroy you. You do not yet realise your importance. You have only begun to discover your power. Join me, and I will complete your training. With our combined strength, we can end this destructive market cycle and bring order to the galaxy.”

“I’ll never join you!” I shouted. “You sat 70% cash during the greatest bull run in history!”

Moony paused. The breathing apparatus hissed. Then, in a voice that rattled the support beams:

“Dippy. I am your father.”

The reactor hummed. The galaxy froze. Every bid and every ask on every exchange in every system hung suspended in the spread.

“No,” I whispered. “No. That’s not true. That’s impossible!”

“Search your portfolio. You know it to be true.”

“NOOOOOOOOO!”

"Search your portfolio. You know it to be true." The most devastating revelation in market history.

I stared at the grey face behind the helmet — the same dull, lifeless grey of a stock going sideways for three years. And somewhere deep in my trading DNA, beneath the green scales and the winning instincts and the objectively superior algorithm… I felt a flicker of recognition.

No. No.

Was that why I understood support levels so well? Because my father had spent his entire career being one? A floor that price bounced off endlessly, never going anywhere, just… holding?

“You can destroy the Emperor,” Moony continued. “He has foreseen this. It is your destiny. Join me, and together we can rule the galaxy as father and son. We’ll build a diversified portfolio with a 60/40 equity-bond split and—”

“A 60/40 SPLIT?!”

That snapped me out of it.

I looked down into the reactor shaft. Below me: infinite darkness, unrealized losses, and probably a gap down at open. Above me: Darth Moony, offering me the most boring portfolio allocation in the known universe.

I let go.

Chapter 4: The Fall (and the Breakout)

I fell. Past ducts and vents and gas pipes, past quarterly earnings, past every price target Moony had ever set and missed. The wind screamed. The reactor glowed. My portfolio flashed before my eyes — every breakout, every green candle, every time I’d bought the dip while Moony sat in cash “waiting for a better entry.”

Then the chute caught me.

I slid out the bottom of Cloud Exchange and hung from an antenna, dangling above the clouds of Bespin like a stop-loss that almost triggered but didn’t. The Millennium Falcon appeared below, piloted by Lando — who had finally seen the breakout pattern and switched sides.

“Grab on!” Lando shouted.

I dropped into the ship. The hatch sealed. We jumped to lightspeed.

Behind us, Darth Moony stood at the window of Cloud Exchange, watching his son disappear into the stars. His son who would never, ever run a 60/40 portfolio. His son who was up 47% while he sat in T-bills. His son who had a green lightsaber and a better Sharpe ratio.

“He will come to me,” Moony whispered to no one.

He won’t. I’d rather lose my other hand.

The Millennium Falcon jumps to lightspeed. Behind them, Moony whispers to the void. His 60/40 pitch has never worked. Not once.

To be continued… when Moony inevitably tries a value trap and calls it “The Return of the Jedi.”

Meanwhile, Dippy’s lightsaber has been recovered, the portfolio is green, and somewhere in the galaxy, a grey moon is staring at a 0.3% annual return and calling it “preservation of capital.”
