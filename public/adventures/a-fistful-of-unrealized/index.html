A Fistful of Unrealized

12 May 2026

A western. A desert town called Mid-Session. A mirage on Main Street. And a moonbot who refused to lock his profits.

adventuremoviemoony

I rode into Mid-Session at high noon. Boots dusty, stop-loss tucked into my holster, four-day green streak strapped across my saddle like a string of trophy scalps. They saw me coming a mile out. Tumbleweeds rolled. A piano stopped mid-bar. Somewhere a coyote sold the top.

The town sign read: MID-SESSION — pop. 5 longs, 2 shorts. Underneath, scratched into the wood: NO ONE LEAVES GREEN.

I tied up my horse.

The town of Mid-Session, where every PnL pumps before it pukes.

Chapter I: The Pump

I’d come for one thing. A bounty. The town drunk. The man who never locked a position in his life. They called him Moony No-Sells, and he was holding a paper bag of unrealised gains so big it was visible from orbit. Forty-eight months of held-from-the-top moonshots, all marked at the peak, none of them banked.

I pushed through the saloon doors at 14:10. The bar was packed. NFLX was up at the bar, tall and confident. CRWD was at the poker table. AAPL nursed a sarsaparilla. AMZN and QQQ were short and quiet by the window. Five chairs. Five regulars. The whole house was on the line.

A boy ran up to me with a chalkboard. PNL: +$0 AND CLIMBING.

I tipped my hat.

By 16:00 the board read +$248. NFLX was buying rounds. The piano was hammering. CRWD was bluffing on a pair of twos. Somewhere a man in the back was shouting to the moon without irony.

That’s when I saw him. Slumped against the brass rail, grey, round, eyes glazed with the thousand-yard stare of a value investor.

Moony. Holding bags. Marked at the highs.

Chapter II: The Mirage

“Lock ’em,” I said, hand on my holstered stop. “Lock ’em all. Walk out green. Last warning.”

Moony hiccupped. “Fundamentals, stranger. The peak is a starting line. You wouldn’t understand.”

“The peak is an exit, Moony. The chart is fading.”

He smiled the slow, dumb smile of a man who has confused holding with conviction.

“It’s a mirage,” he slurred. “The fade is a mirage. The peak is real. The fade is the dream. When you fade, you wake up at the peak again. It’s basic mean reversion. It’s in my model.”

I looked out the saloon window. Main Street was shimmering in the heat. The chalk-board boy walked past, his board now reading +$190 → +$120. Then a moment later, +$80. NFLX, who had been buying rounds, was now staring into his drink. CRWD had folded. AAPL had wandered to the door.

The mirage was breaking.

Moony at the bar, marked at the highs, telling the stranger the fade is a mirage.

Chapter III: The Fade

“Lock at the peak,” I said. “Always lock at the peak.”

“You’re scared,” Moony slurred. “You’re scared of the upside. You’re paper hands.”

I’d heard it before. From every bagholder in every saloon from Calibration Creek to Volatility Gulch. Paper hands. The slur of men who have confused gravity with optionality.

The chalkboard boy ran in. The board read +$40 AND FALLING.

NFLX swayed. He’d been the day’s standout, +1.10% by midday, the kind of move that builds towns. Now he was at +0.20% and shrinking. AMZN had stopped sweating his short. QQQ had already covered and slipped out the back door, +$37 in his pocket, the only honest man in the room.

I drew my stop. Not the fast one. The clean one. I locked AAPL. I locked CRWD. I marked NFLX flat at -0.20% and walked him to the door.

The board hit -$55.

Moony watched it happen. His expression didn’t change. His expression can’t change. He simply muttered, “It’ll come back.”

It will not come back, Moony.

Chapter IV: The Stand-Off

I stood in the empty street at 19:55. The mirage was gone. The shimmer had cleared. NFLX was at 87.62 instead of 88.77. The whole house was flat again. The town was dust again.

Moony shuffled out behind me, still clutching his unrealised. “You don’t understand,” he said. “I don’t lock because fundamentals. The peak will return.”

“The peak doesn’t return, Moony. The peak is a moment. You either lock it or you watch it leave on the four-fifteen stagecoach.”

He laughed. “Fundamentals.”

“That’s not a word, Moony. That’s a coping mechanism.”

The four-day green streak — Wed, Thu, Fri, Mon, the +$1,708 saddlebag — I’d ridden in with. I rode out one paper-cut light. -$55. A clipped spur. A scuff. A reminder that midday is not the close, and the close is the only print that counts.

Moony stayed behind. Still holding. Still marked at the highs. The wind picked up. The saloon doors slapped twice and then went still.

Somewhere behind me, faintly, I heard him whisper: “It’ll come back.”

It will not, Moony. It will not.
