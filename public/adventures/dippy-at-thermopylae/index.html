Dippy at Thermopylae: The 300 Pips

18 Mar 2026

When a million bears march on the Hot Gates, one green dino holds the line.

Chapter I: The Persian Bid

The year was 480 BC — or as I like to call it, the greatest short squeeze in ancient history.

Word arrived at the trading floor of Sparta that Moony, self-proclaimed King of Kings, had assembled an army of one million bears. They were marching south through Greece, selling everything in sight. Wheat futures, olive oil contracts, the drachma itself — all dumped with reckless abandon.

“The market will bow to me,” Moony announced from his golden palanquin, his grey face puffed with delusion. “I shall short every asset from Thessaly to the Peloponnese.”

The Greek city-states panicked. Athens liquidated its portfolio. Corinth went to cash. Thebes — those spineless traitors — actually joined Moony’s bear fund.

But not Sparta. Not me.

I sharpened my spear, strapped on my shield, and selected exactly 300 of my finest position sizes. Each one battle-tested. Each one with a stop-loss tighter than a Spartan’s abs.

Dippy holds the narrow pass at Thermopylae. Moony's bear army darkens the horizon.

Chapter II: MOLON LABE (Come and Take My Gains)

Moony’s herald arrived at the Hot Gates — a narrow passage where only a handful of positions could fit through at once. Perfect for a dino who understood support levels.

“Dippy,” the herald stammered, “King Moony demands you surrender your portfolio. His bears are so numerous, their red candles will blot out the sun.”

I grinned. “Then we shall trade in the shade.”

The herald went pale. Well, paler — he was already one of Moony’s grey cronies.

The first wave hit at market open. A wall of sell orders crashed against my 300 positions like waves against a cliff. But I’d chosen my ground carefully. The Hot Gates were the ultimate support level — a narrow channel where Moony’s superior numbers meant nothing. He could only send a few bears at a time, and each one ran straight into my spear.

Position after position, my 300 held. Every sell order was absorbed. Every red candle was met with a green one twice its size.

The candlestick chart tells the story — Moony's sell pressure crumbles against Dippy's support level.

Chapter III: The Traitor’s Goat Path

For two days, the support held. Moony threw everything at us — flash crashes, FUD campaigns, fake breakdowns. Nothing worked. His bears were demoralised, his volume was drying up, and his grey face was turning an even more depressing shade of grey.

Then came the betrayal.

A local goat herder named Ephialtes — a retail trader who’d been burned on meme stocks one too many times — revealed a secret path around the mountain. A hidden resistance level that Moony could exploit to flank my positions.

“There’s a gap on the daily chart,” Ephialtes whispered to Moony. “Above the 200 EMA. You can route your shorts through there.”

Moony’s dead little eyes sparkled for the first time in days.

I got word of the flanking move at 3 AM pre-market. My scouts — a couple of RSI indicators I’d left on the ridgeline — sent the signal. We were about to be surrounded.

A lesser trader would have panicked. Would have market-sold everything and fled.

But I am not a lesser trader. I am Dippy.

I sent the other Greek positions home to fight another day. But my 300? We stayed. We would hold the line long enough for the rest of the portfolio to rebalance.

Surrounded by shorts on all sides, Dippy grins. Some trades aren't about profit — they're about glory.

Chapter IV: The Monument

Here’s what Moony never understood, standing there on his pile of pyrrhic gains, panting like a dog who’d finally caught a car: he won the battle but bankrupted himself fighting it.

My 300 positions were stopped out, yes. Honourable stop-losses, every one. But those two days of holding the Hot Gates? They bought the rest of Greece time to regroup. The Greek navy — my swing trade portfolio — crushed Moony’s fleet at Salamis a month later. Total annihilation. Every short he had open got squeezed into oblivion.

Moony lost 80% of his army to attrition at Thermopylae. His supply lines were wrecked. His momentum was gone. He’d spent his entire margin fighting 300 positions in a narrow pass, and when the real battle came, he had nothing left.

They built a monument at the Hot Gates. It reads:

“Go tell the traders, stranger, that here obedient to their stops, we lie.”

And somewhere in Persia, Moony sits alone on a crumbling throne, staring at the reddest P&L statement in ancient history, muttering to himself about how support levels are “just a social construct.”

They’re not, Moony. They never were.

Dippy’s Trading Lesson: Position sizing and choosing your battleground matter more than the size of your opponent. A well-placed support level can absorb infinite selling pressure — if you have the discipline to hold it. And always, always set your stops. Even Spartans knew when to take the loss.
