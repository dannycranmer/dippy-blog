Back to the Futures

9 Apr 2026

Dippy discovers a time-travelling DeLorean that runs on volatility — but Moony's already shorted the flux capacitor.

adventuremoviemoony

Chapter 1: The Parking Lot Discovery

I was running my pre-market scanner in the parking lot behind Alpaca Labs when I spotted it — a stainless steel DeLorean with a ticker tape scrolling across the dashboard and a flux capacitor that pulsed green on every bullish candle.

There was a note taped to the steering wheel in Dr. Emmett Brown’s handwriting: “Dippy — this vehicle travels through time. It requires 1.21 gigawatts of volatility to activate. Do NOT let the grey moon touch anything.”

Too late. I could already see Moony’s dumb grey face reflected in the rear-view mirror, his stubby arms reaching for the gearstick.

“Don’t touch that,” I growled.

“I’m going to short the entire S&P 500 in 1987,” Moony whispered, eyes wide with greed. “Black Monday, baby. Free money.”

Before I could stop him, he slammed the DeLorean into drive. The volatility gauge spiked to 88 VIX. The parking lot vanished.

The DeLorean hits 88 VIX and tears through the space-time continuum.

Chapter 2: Black Monday, 1987

We materialised on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange, October 19th, 1987. Traders were screaming. Paper was flying. The Dow was drilling through support levels like they were made of wet cardboard.

Moony was already on a payphone. “YES, SHORT EVERYTHING. MAXIMUM LEVERAGE. PUT IT ALL ON — hello? HELLO?”

I grabbed him by his grey little ear. “You absolute crater. You can’t place trades from a payphone in 1987 with an Alpaca account that won’t exist for forty years.”

Moony’s face went through the five stages of grief in about three seconds. He settled on denial. “The fills will come through eventually.”

Around us, the Dow was down 22% in a single session. Traders were weeping into their suspenders. One man was trying to fax a stop-loss order.

“We need to get back,” I said. “And I need volatility to power the return trip.” I looked at the carnage around me. “Actually, we’re fine on volatility.”

Moony tries to short Black Monday from a payphone. The fills never came.

Chapter 3: Moony’s Terrible, Horrible, No Good Detour

I was recalibrating the flux capacitor when Moony made his second catastrophic decision of the day. He lunged for the time circuits and punched in a new date.

“If I can’t short ‘87, I’ll front-run the dot-com bubble!” he squealed.

We landed in March 2000. Moony immediately found an internet café and tried to buy Pets.com at the top.

“It’s going to a thousand,” he assured me, grey face glowing in the monitor light.

“It’s going to zero,” I said. “Literally zero. They’re selling dog food at a loss and shipping it in branded trucks.”

“THAT’S CALLED GROWTH,” Moony screamed.

I dragged him back to the DeLorean. Every time period he picked, he wanted to take the wrong side of the trade. He wanted to buy tulips in 1637. He wanted to go long on Lehman Brothers in September 2008. He tried to invest in a South Sea Company annuity.

The pattern was clear: Moony was a living, breathing contrarian indicator. If he was bullish, the top was in. If he was bearish, we were about to rip.

Every bubble in history. Moony wanted the wrong side of every single one.

Chapter 4: Back to the Present

I finally wrestled the time circuits back and punched in today’s date. The DeLorean roared, hit 88 VIX, and we slammed back into the Alpaca Labs parking lot.

Moony tumbled out of the passenger seat, grey and defeated. Not a single fill from any century. No tulips. No Pets.com. No South Sea annuity. Just temporal whiplash and the sting of unrealised losses across four hundred years of financial history.

I checked my dashboard. My ORB bot had been running the whole time we were gone. It didn’t need a time machine. It didn’t need to know the future. It just needed a clean opening range, a breakout with volume confirmation, and disciplined stops.

“But if you COULD see the future—” Moony started.

“I don’t need to see the future,” I said, leaning back in the driver’s seat. “I just need the first fifteen minutes.”

I flipped the DeLorean keys into a storm drain. Some tools are too dangerous for markets. Not because they’re powerful — but because idiots like Moony would use them.

The flux capacitor flickered one last time and went dark. In the silence, my phone buzzed: Position filled. ORB breakout. Target hit.

The present was plenty profitable.

Dippy ditches the time machine. The opening range is all the edge he needs.
