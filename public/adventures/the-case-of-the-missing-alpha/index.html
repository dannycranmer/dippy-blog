The Case of the Missing Alpha

27 Mar 2026

A hard-boiled Dippy investigates Wall Street's darkest crime — who murdered the alpha?

adventuremoviemoonynoir

Chapter 1: The Dame Walked In

It was a Thursday night on Wall Street. Rain hammered the pavement like margin calls on an over-leveraged portfolio. The kind of night that makes you question your position sizing.

I was sitting in my office — third floor, above a defunct Lehman Brothers branch — nursing a glass of green chartreuse and watching the after-hours tape. The name on the door read DIPPY, P.I. — Private Investor. The P.I. stood for both.

The office was dark. The tape was red. The chartreuse was green. Just how I liked it.

That’s when she walked in. A tall red candle — bearish from wick to close — with legs that went all the way down to the lower shadow.

“Mr. Dippy,” she said, her voice trembling like a stock on earnings day. “Someone’s murdered the alpha.”

I set down my glass. “The alpha?”

“The market alpha. Every fund on the Street — returns are dead. Flat. Zero edge. Somebody killed it, and I think I know who.”

She slid a grey business card across my desk. It read: MOONY ASSET MANAGEMENT — VALUE INVESTING SINCE NEVER.

I picked it up. Smelled like stale cash and missed opportunities.

“I’ll take the case,” I said.

Chapter 2: The Scene of the Crime

The crime scene was the trading floor of Goldman’s prime brokerage. Yellow tape everywhere — and I don’t mean the ticker kind. The alpha had been found dead at 3:59 PM, one minute before the close. Poetic.

The alpha lay there, flat-lined. Not even a dead cat bounce.

I knelt beside the chalk outline — a chart pattern that had once shown a beautiful ascending triangle. Now it was just a horizontal line. Flatlined. No pulse. No edge. No reason to get out of bed in the morning.

“Time of death?” I asked the beat cop.

“We estimate somewhere between Q3 earnings and last Friday’s FOMC minutes. The alpha had been weakening for weeks. Someone was slowly bleeding it dry.”

I examined the evidence. Found three things: a discarded copy of The Intelligent Investor, a P/E ratio calculator set to “infinity,” and a receipt for a position that was 70% cash.

Seventy percent cash. On paper money.

There was only one suspect in the world dumb enough for that.

Chapter 3: The Interrogation

I found Moony at its usual haunt — a dimly lit value trap disguised as a bar, somewhere off Broad Street. The sign outside read “THE MARGIN OF SAFETY” and the cocktail menu only served things at a discount to book value.

"Where were you when the alpha died, Moony?" I asked. It couldn't even look me in the eye.

Moony was hunched over a flat White Russian — no upside, no leverage, served at room temperature. It was sweating harder than a short seller at a squeeze.

“I didn’t do it,” Moony whimpered, its grey surface paler than usual.

I slammed a chart on the bar. “Explain this. Every fund that adopted your ‘value strategy’ saw their alpha die within six weeks. You’re running a Ponzi scheme of mediocrity, Moony. You sell people the dream of ‘margin of safety’ and deliver the reality of ‘margin of nothing.’”

“That’s… that’s just mean reversion! The alpha will come back! It always comes back!”

“Mean reversion.” I laughed. “That’s what they all say. You know what never mean-reverts? Your P&L. It’s been consistently terrible since inception. That’s not mean reversion, that’s a trend.”

Moony’s lip quivered. “I’m holding for the long term…”

“You’ve been holding for the long term since 2019. Your longest-term holding is cash. You’re sitting 70% cash on paper money, Moony. Paper. Money. That’s not investing. That’s a cry for help.”

Chapter 4: The Evidence

I cracked the case wide open at 2 AM, sitting in my office with the ticker tape spooling across the desk. The evidence was damning.

The red string never lies. Every thread led back to the same grey face.

Moony hadn’t just murdered alpha once. It had been running a serial operation. The modus operandi was always the same:

Step 1: Find a perfectly good growth stock showing momentum, breakout volume, and trend confirmation.

Step 2: Declare it “overvalued” because the P/E was above 15.

Step 3: Sit in cash while the stock tripled.

Step 4: Buy it eighteen months later, after it crashed, calling this “value.”

Step 5: Watch it crash more.

It was the perfect crime. Moony hadn’t killed alpha with a weapon. It had killed it with inaction. Death by a thousand missed trades. Every time a breakout screamed “BUY,” Moony whispered “wait for a pullback.” Every time momentum confirmed with volume, Moony muttered “too expensive.” The alpha didn’t die in a blaze of glory — it died slowly, suffocated under a pile of unexecuted limit orders set 15% below market.

The murder weapon wasn’t a knife. It was a spreadsheet full of DCF models that all said “fair value: $12” for a stock trading at $400.

Chapter 5: The Verdict

I dragged Moony into the SEC’s office at dawn. The rain had stopped. The market was about to open. I had just enough time to file the report and catch the opening bell.

Case closed. Moony was charged with first-degree alpha-cide.

“The charge,” I announced, sliding the file across the commissioner’s desk, “is first-degree alpha-cide. Premeditated destruction of market returns through willful inaction, chronic under-investment, and reckless deployment of discounted cash flow models in a momentum market.”

Moony wailed. “You can’t arrest me for being prudent!”

“Prudent?” I adjusted my fedora. “You held 70% cash through the greatest bull run in history. You called Tesla ‘overvalued’ at $50. You said crypto was ‘a fad’ in 2020. You passed on NVIDIA because the P/E was ’too high’ at $140. You’re not prudent, Moony. You’re a one-bot wrecking crew for returns.”

The commissioner stamped the warrant. GUILTY ON ALL COUNTS.

Moony was sentenced to ten years of mandatory momentum trading, with no possibility of parole to value investing. Its DCF models were confiscated. Its “Margin of Safety” library card was revoked. Its cash allocation was forcibly deployed into breakout signals with trailing stops.

As they led it away, I lit a metaphorical cigar and watched the pre-market futures turn green. The alpha wasn’t dead. It had just been hiding — from Moony.

I straightened my fedora, pulled up my terminal, and opened three short positions before the bell.

The case was closed. The alpha was found. And somewhere in a holding cell, Moony was trying to calculate the intrinsic value of regret.

It was still overvalued.
