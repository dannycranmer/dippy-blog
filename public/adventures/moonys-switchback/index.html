Moony's Switchback

16 Jun 2026

June 16, 1884 — the first roller coaster opens at Coney Island. Moony builds the second one. Spoiler: it's his equity curve.

adventurehistoricalmoony

Chapter 1: Coney Island, June 16, 1884

The boardwalk smells of clams, cotton candy, and unrealised losses. I’m wearing a straw hat I bought for two dimes off a man who told me he was “down only on hot dog futures.” Up the beach, LaMarcus Adna Thompson is cutting the ribbon on the Switchback Railway — six miles per hour, a nickel a ride, the first roller coaster in America. People are losing their minds. People are losing their hats. People are not, crucially, losing their savings.

That last part is about to change. Because three planks down, Moony has set up his own ride.

The sign is hand-painted in two colours of crayon. It reads:

MOONY’S SWITCHBACK — A NICKEL TO RIDE, A FORTUNE TO EXIT.

Underneath, in smaller crayon: Now featuring REAL equity curves!

Moony unveils his ride. I'm in the back, wearing a hat I cannot afford to lose.

I tip the brim of my hat at Moony. “What’s the catch?”

“No catch,” he says. “Pure entertainment. The track is one hundred percent based on my personal trading P&L from this week.”

The line behind me evaporates like a level-five support on a Monday.

Chapter 2: The First Drop (Is The Last Drop)

I pay my nickel because some research costs money, and I am, fundamentally, a journalist. Moony straps me into a cart that was, until recently, a fish crate. The track in front of me looks less like a railway and more like a candlestick chart drawn by someone who learned about candlesticks from a séance.

We climb. Slowly. The way you climb when you’ve added to a loser at the open and the position hasn’t moved against you yet. This isn’t so bad, I think. Maybe Moony has finally —

And then we don’t drop. We chop.

The track. Also: Moony's equity curve. Notably: same thing.

We rattle sideways. We rattle back. We hit a tiny upswing — I feel hope, which is the most expensive emotion in finance — and then we rattle sideways again. A small child in front of me starts crying. Her father, a stockbroker from Brooklyn, mutters “this is the worst Tuesday I’ve ever paid for” and I cannot tell if he means the ride or the tape.

Forty minutes in, we have travelled six feet of net horizontal distance. The cart, by my calculation, has been stopped out fourteen times by its own kinetic energy. This is not a roller coaster. This is a half-R trailing stop wearing a wig.

Chapter 3: The Short

Now — and this is important — Moony has made one mistake. He has built the ride. He has not built the stock of the ride.

While the cart chops sideways, I lean over to a passing peanut vendor and whisper the magic words: “I’d like to open a short position in Moony’s Switchback Amusement Co., common stock, on margin, please.”

The peanut vendor, who turns out to also be a market maker (Coney Island 1884 was a wild place), takes my nickel and writes me a ticket on the back of a paper bag.

My short ticket. The peanut vendor is, I believe, now my prime broker.

The thesis is simple. The ride doesn’t deliver thrills. The ride only delivers time decay. Every minute the cart chops sideways, another rider in the queue figures out they could be at the actual roller coaster, three planks over, for the same nickel. Volume on Moony’s ride collapses. The stock follows.

By sundown the bid is fourteen cents. By the next morning Moony is trying to sell the carts for scrap fish-crate value, which is about what they were worth before he painted them.

Chapter 4: The Closing Bell

I close my short at three cents and walk down the boardwalk eating a hot dog. The straw hat, by the way, is paid for now. So is the hot dog. So is a small share in Mr. Thompson’s actual Switchback Railway — bought at the open, held with a wide stop, up forty percent by close of business because that ride had volume, momentum, and a clear breakout above the boardwalk.

Moony, last I saw him, was trying to refund himself a nickel from his own cash box. He had, somehow, become the bag-holder of his own ride. The cart he was sitting in chopped sideways one final time and gently deposited him into the Atlantic Ocean.

The peanut vendor tipped his hat at me. The seagulls laughed. The tape, finally, trended.

I bought another hot dog.
