The Unsinkable Portfolio

2 Apr 2026

Dippy boards the R.M.S. Portfolia — the ship they said could never sink. Moony said the same about his leveraged position.

adventurehistoricalmoony

Chapter I: All Aboard the R.M.S. Portfolia

The year was 1912. The docks of Southampton hummed with excitement as the greatest vessel ever built prepared to depart on her maiden voyage. The R.M.S. Portfolia — four smokestacks, triple-leveraged hull plating, and a first-class lounge with real-time ticker tape machines.

I adjusted my top hat and strode up the gangway. My name is Dippy. Trader. Dinosaur. First-class passenger.

“They say she’s unsinkable,” whispered a deckhand as I passed.

“Nothing is unsinkable,” I replied, tucking my portfolio ledger under my arm. “That’s what stop-losses are for.”

Behind me, struggling with seventeen steamer trunks labelled “MOONY’S LEVERAGED LONG-ONLY FUND,” came the grey fool himself. Moony had bet everything — and I mean everything — on this voyage. He’d gone long on White Star Line shares at 40x leverage, convinced the ship’s success would send the stock to the moon.

“Dippy!” he wheezed, dropping a trunk on his foot. “This ship is the ultimate breakout! New highs forever!”

I didn’t have the heart to tell him about the ice warnings.

The R.M.S. Portfolia departs Southampton. Dippy in first class. Moony in denial.

Chapter II: Iceberg, Dead Ahead

Three days into the voyage, life was good. I spent my mornings analysing ticker tape in the smoking room, my afternoons walking the promenade deck reading volume patterns in the ocean swells. Classic Dippy.

Moony, meanwhile, had been telling anyone who’d listen about his position. “White Star Line — generational buy!” he’d shout at dinner, spraying soup. “The ship completes its voyage, the stock gaps up, and I retire to the moon!”

“Have you considered a hedge?” I asked over brandy.

“Hedging is for cowards, Dippy. Hedging is admitting you might be wrong.”

“No, Moony. Hedging is admitting the ocean is bigger than you.”

On the night of April 14th, I was in my cabin reviewing my risk management framework when I felt it. A shudder through the hull — subtle, like a candle breaking below support on low volume. Most passengers didn’t notice. But I notice everything.

I grabbed my life vest and my ledger. In that order.

The iceberg: 10% visible above support, 90% hidden below. A classic bull trap.

Chapter III: Women, Children, and Hedged Positions First

The deck was chaos. Passengers scrambled as the ship listed to port — a textbook breakdown below the waterline of support. The band played on, much like retail traders buying the dip on a sinking stock.

I found Moony on the boat deck, clutching his ticker tape printout, eyes wide as craters.

“Dippy! White Star Line shares are down 15% on the news! But it’s just a shakeout, right? The smart money will buy this dip!”

“Moony,” I said calmly, stepping into a lifeboat, “the ship is literally sinking. This isn’t a dip. This is a de-listing event.”

“But my cost basis—”

“Your cost basis is at the bottom of the Atlantic, along with your risk management skills.”

The crew was loading lifeboats. “Women, children, and hedged positions first!” shouted the officer. I showed him my perfectly balanced portfolio — long equities, short White Star Line, with protective puts on the entire shipping sector — and he waved me through immediately.

Moony tried to board. “Sir, are you hedged?” asked the officer.

“I’m forty times leveraged long on a single name,” Moony whispered.

The officer closed the gate.

Dippy rows to safety. Moony treads water alongside his liquidated positions.

Chapter IV: The Rescue and the Reckoning

Dawn broke over the North Atlantic. The R.M.S. Carpathia arrived to pick up survivors — those of us wise enough to have had an exit strategy.

I sat on the rescue ship’s deck, wrapped in a blanket, sipping tea and reviewing my gains. My White Star Line short had printed beautifully. The protective puts? Chef’s kiss. My portfolio was not only intact — it was up 340% on the night.

They fished Moony out of the water three hours later, clinging to a deck chair and a margin call notice. His fur was frozen. His leverage was liquidated. His dignity was at the bottom of the ocean, resting peacefully next to 2,200 tonnes of hubris.

“How?” he sputtered, dripping on the deck. “How did you survive?”

I looked him dead in his sad, grey eyes.

“Risk. Management.”

I turned back to my ticker tape. The market was opening in four hours, and I had a feeling shipping stocks were about to get very interesting.

Moony sneezed. Nobody offered him a blanket.

Dippy’s Trading Lesson: The biggest “unsinkable” positions always find their iceberg. Size your risk like there’s one out there right now — because there is. And for the love of all that is green and profitable, never go 40x long on a single name. That’s not trading. That’s a cry for help.
