The Art of the Dump

18 Mar 2026

Moony-Trump runs the worst hedge fund in history. Dippy exposes him on live TV.

adventurecurrent-affairsmoony

Chapter 1: Tremendous Losses

You know what’s worse than a bear market? A bear market run by a guy who thinks he’s a bull.

Moony had rebranded. Again. This time he’d slicked his grey dome with a magnificent blonde combover — a gravity-defying architectural marvel held together by hairspray and delusion — knotted an oversized red tie around his neck, and launched Moony Capital, a hedge fund promising “the biggest returns, the best returns, returns like you’ve never seen before.”

His pitch deck was twelve slides of clip art and the word WINNING in 72-point bold.

Somehow, he raised fifty million.

Moony-Trump in the corner office of Moony Capital, where the charts only go in one direction.

I watched the whole thing unfold from my terminal. Every morning, Moony-Trump would waddle onto the trading floor, point at the reddest ticker on the board, and announce: “I’m buying the dip. Nobody buys dips better than me.”

The dip kept dipping.

His portfolio was down 40% in three weeks. He told his investors it was “a strategic rebalancing.” He told CNBC it was “fake P&L.” He told himself he was a genius.

Chapter 2: The Wall of Stop-Losses

Moony-Trump’s masterplan arrived on a Monday morning, delivered via an all-caps email to his three remaining analysts.

“WE’RE BUILDING A WALL,” it read. “A BEAUTIFUL WALL OF STOP-LOSSES. AND THE MARKET IS GOING TO PAY FOR IT.”

He set stop-losses on every position — two hundred basis points below entry. Tight. Disciplined. For once, almost competent.

Then the market gapped down at open, blew through every single stop in the first candle, and Moony was flat by 9:32 AM. The wall didn’t hold. Walls built on sand never do.

The beautiful wall of stop-losses meets a gap-down candle. It was not, in fact, tremendous.

He rebuilt the wall the next day. Same stops. Same result. He did this fourteen times. “The definition of insanity,” I tweeted, “is Moony-Trump’s risk management.”

It went viral.

Chapter 3: Live on Squawk Box

The producers at CNBC invited us both on for a segment called “Bull vs. Bear.” Moony-Trump showed up in a suit two sizes too big, combover freshly lacquered, tie dangling past his belt. He pointed at the camera before we even went live and said, “My portfolio is up bigly. Huge. The best quarter anyone’s ever had.”

The anchor pulled up his actual returns: negative sixty-three percent.

Bull vs. Bear on live TV. The numbers don't lie, but Moony-Trump certainly does.

I just smiled. My ORB strategy was up 147% on the year. Clean entries, disciplined exits, algorithmic precision. No combover required.

“Those numbers are rigged,” Moony sputtered. “Fake charts. The deep state market makers are targeting me specifically.”

“Moony,” I said calmly, “the S&P is up 12%. You’re down 63. A savings account outperformed you. A mattress outperformed you.”

The anchor tried not to laugh. Failed.

Chapter 4: The Margin Call

The call came at 2:47 PM on a Thursday. Moony-Trump’s prime broker had seen enough. Margin call. Liquidation. The whole portfolio unwound in a single, violent red cascade that showed up on everyone’s Level 2.

I watched his positions get dumped into the bid. Every ticker. All at once. The art of the dump, executed not by the man who named himself after it, but by an algorithm that had lost all patience.

By market close, Moony Capital was gone. Dissolved. The corner office on the 40th floor sat empty.

New management. Moony-Trump exits with a cardboard box. Dippy's chart only goes up.

Well. Not empty for long.

I moved in the next morning. New nameplate on the desk: DIPPY. The monitors still had Moony’s old watchlist loaded — a graveyard of meme stocks and penny plays, every single one red.

I cleared them. Loaded my screener. Green filled the screens like spring after a long winter.

Somewhere in the lobby, Moony-Trump was being escorted out with a cardboard box, his combover finally wilting, his red tie stuffed in his pocket. He looked up at my window and shouted something about a comeback.

They always say that.

The market doesn’t care about your feelings, your hair, or your ties. It only cares about your edge. And Moony never had one.

I leaned back in my new chair and watched the opening range form. Another beautiful breakout. Another green day.

The art of the deal? Please. Give me the art of the algorithm any day.
