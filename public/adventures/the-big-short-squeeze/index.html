The Big Short Squeeze

21 Mar 2026

A hard-boiled detective story where Dippy investigates Moony's biggest con — a short position the size of a small country.

adventuremoviemoony

Chapter 1: The Dame with the Margin Call

It was a Thursday. The kind of Thursday where the VIX reads 35 and every candle on the chart looks like a cry for help. I was in my office — third floor of the Nasdaq building, above a deli that only takes Bitcoin — when she walked in.

She had legs like a yield curve and eyes that said “inverted.” Her name was Janet. Janet Yield.

“Mr. Dippy,” she said, sliding a manila folder across my desk. “Someone is shorting the entire semiconductor sector. Naked. No hedge. No stop loss.” She paused. “Forty million dollars.”

I opened the folder. The short seller’s account was registered to a shell company called Lunar Capital LLC. The logo was a grey circle with a sad face.

I knew that face.

The dame with the margin call walked into my office on a Thursday that smelled like volatility.

Chapter 2: Following the Paper Trail

I hit the streets. First stop: the options desk at Goldman, where a guy named Vinny owed me a favour since I tipped him off about the NVDA breakout at $180.

“Lunar Capital,” Vinny said, pulling up the flow. “Selling calls naked on AMD, AVGO, NVDA. All semis. Opened the position three weeks ago when NVDA was at $190. No stop. No protective calls. Just pure, unhedged conviction that semiconductors were going to zero.”

“That’s not a trade,” I said. “That’s a suicide note with a Bloomberg terminal.”

Vinny shrugged. “Gets worse. They doubled down after each earnings beat. AMD reports blowout numbers? They sell more calls. AVGO raises guidance? More calls. It’s like they’re allergic to being right.”

I pulled up the chart on my phone. Semi index was up 14% since the position opened. Lunar Capital was sitting on a drawdown the size of a small European nation’s GDP. And with the PCE number coming Friday and Fed hawks circling, the squeeze could go nuclear.

“Who’s the beneficial owner?” I asked.

Vinny slid me a napkin. On it was a name I’d been chasing for years.

M. Oony. Registered address: 70 Cash Street, Bagholding, Connecticut.

Moony had been doubling down on shorts every time semiconductors rallied. The chart told the whole story.

Chapter 3: The Confrontation

I found Moony in his usual spot — a basement office on Wall Street with a Bloomberg terminal running Windows XP and a motivational poster that said “CASH IS KING” in Comic Sans. He was 70% cash, as usual. The other 30% was on fire.

“Moony,” I said, kicking the door open. “Naked short on the entire semi sector? No stop loss? What are you, a strategy or a cry for help?”

Moony spun around in his chair. His grey face was the colour of a stock after a bankruptcy filing. “It’s a value play!” he squeaked. “Semiconductors are overvalued! The P/E ratios are—”

“The P/E ratios are irrelevant when NVDA is printing money faster than the Fed, you absolute donut.” I slammed the folder on his desk. “You’re down five million. AMD just reported record data centre revenue. AVGO raised guidance for the third time. And you DOUBLED YOUR SHORT?”

“It’ll come back,” Moony whispered, staring at his screen. “Value always wins in the long run.”

“The long run?” I laughed. “Moony, your margin call arrives in forty-five minutes. The only thing coming back is your lunch.”

I pulled up a chair. Somewhere outside, a ticker tape was printing green. Always green. That’s the thing about momentum — it doesn’t care about your thesis. It doesn’t care about your P/E models or your DCF spreadsheets. Momentum is a freight train, and Moony had tied himself to the tracks and called it “conviction.”

Moony's office: Comic Sans posters, Windows XP, and a portfolio redder than a traffic light.

Chapter 4: The Squeeze

The margin call hit at 2:47 PM. I watched it happen from the trading floor — a forced buy-in on every short position Moony held. The covering orders hit the tape like a green tsunami. AMD spiked 3% in four minutes. NVDA broke through resistance like it had somewhere better to be. AVGO gapped up so hard the chart needed a chiropractor.

Moony’s liquidation was so violent it showed up on the tape as a single green candle the size of a skyscraper. The quants called it “The Moony Candle.” It would be studied in risk management textbooks for decades.

I stood at the window, watching the numbers climb. My own long positions — carefully sized, properly hedged, with trailing stops at 0.75R because I’m not an animal — were printing money. The semi sector was ripping, and every dollar of Moony’s forced covering was fuel for the fire.

My phone buzzed. A text from Janet Yield: “Case closed?”

I typed back: “The case was never open. Moony’s the kind of trader who brings a P/E ratio to a momentum fight. The market doesn’t care about your models. It cares about your stops.”

I pocketed the phone and lit a metaphorical cigar. Somewhere across town, Moony was explaining to his compliance officer why he’d lost forty million dollars shorting the strongest sector in the market with no risk management.

The ticker tape kept printing. Green. Always green.

That’s the thing about this business, kid. It’s not about being right. It’s about being sized right. And Moony? Moony was sized like a clown at a funeral — technically present, but nobody wanted him there.

The Moony Candle: a forced liquidation so violent it'd be studied in textbooks for decades.

Case closed. Position closed. Moony’s account — also closed.
