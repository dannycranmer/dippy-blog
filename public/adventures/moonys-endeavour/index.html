Moony's Endeavour

11 Jun 2026

June 11, 1770: Captain Moony charts a course straight into the Great Barrier Reef of resistance. The cannons go overboard. The cope stays aboard.

adventurehistoricalmoony

Chapter One — All Sail, No Stops

The year is 1770. The HMS Endeavour slides out of Sydney harbour with a full hold, a full crew, and a captain with absolutely no risk parameters. Captain Moony stands on the quarterdeck in a tricorn hat two sizes too big, telescope pointed at the horizon, muttering about “uncharted upside.”

I’m the lookout. Green dino in the crow’s nest, chart in one claw, stop-loss ticket in the other. Below me the deck is groaning under cargo Moony loaded at the top of last week: forty bales of MEME silk, two crates of unrealised AMC, and a single barrel labelled “Cope — Naval Issue.”

“Sailing master!” Moony bellows. “What’s the trend?”

“Trend’s down, captain,” I shout back. “Volume’s drying up. The barometer is screaming. Maybe we reef the sails.”

He squints into the telescope. “Negative, dino. We are averaging in on the new world.”

Captain Moony departs Sydney harbour with the HODL banner flying high. The crow's nest already disagrees.

Chapter Two — Reef Sighted

By nightfall on June 11, the chart in my claws is screaming. There’s a wall of coral marked clearly in red ink: “GREAT BARRIER REEF OF RESISTANCE — DO NOT CROSS WITHOUT VOLUME.”

I cup my snout and bellow down: “CAPTAIN! Hard to starboard! Resistance dead ahead!”

Moony is below decks holding a candle to a chart of his own. His chart has been redrawn with crayon. The reef has been relabelled “minor pullback opportunity.” The depth soundings have all been crossed out and replaced with the word “vibes.”

“Steady as she goes!” he shouts up. “If we just hold through this little chop, the next leg is Tahiti.”

The lookout’s job is to call the level. I called it. Moony ignored it. The next sound is wood meeting coral at sixteen knots.

Resistance level dead ahead, redrawn in crayon as a "minor pullback." The crow's nest dissents.

Chapter Three — Overboard With the Cargo

CRUNCH.

The Endeavour shudders. Coral punches through the hull at the waterline. Saltwater pours into the hold and starts dissolving the MEME silk. Moony staggers up on deck, hat askew, eyes wild.

“Captain, we have to lighten the ship!” the bosun screams. “Throw the cannons over!”

Moony hesitates. The cannons cost a fortune. They are the core position. But the ship is settling fast. One by one, six tonnes of iron go over the side: cannons, anchors, the spare rum, the entire crate of unrealised AMC. The Endeavour groans, rises an inch, and finally floats free of the reef on the next high tide.

Moony stands at the rail watching his cargo sink. The thing about throwing it overboard at the bottom is that you don’t get to call it risk management. You get to call it capitulation. The reef doesn’t care which word you pick.

Six tonnes of core position go over the rail at the absolute low. The ship floats. The bag-holder weeps.

Chapter Four — The Logbook Lies

We limp into a river mouth for repairs. Forty-eight days of careening, scraping coral out of the timbers, patching holes with our own shirts. The crew is half-mad. The rum is gone. The cope is plentiful.

Moony sits in the captain’s cabin writing his official journal. I peek over his shoulder. He has titled the entry: “On the Felicitous Discovery of a New Reef, and Other Strategic Manoeuvres.”

The reef is now described as “intentional reconnaissance.” The lost cannons are “deployed to the seabed for future recovery.” The flooded hold is “a planned drawdown phase.” There is no mention of the lookout who called the level three hours early. There is no mention of the chart in crayon.

I am no longer aboard. I jumped ship at the reef with my own little dinghy, a short on coral futures, and a tight stop. By the time Moony’s Endeavour limps home a year late with a hull full of barnacles and an empty hold, my dinghy is moored in Tahiti with a bar tab full of paper umbrellas.

The lookout always wins. The captain who ignores the lookout always writes a beautiful logbook about how he meant to do that.

Dippy in Tahiti with the cocktail. Moony, on the horizon, still writing the logbook.

Moony lost again. As is tradition.
