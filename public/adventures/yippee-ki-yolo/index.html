Yippee-Ki-YOLO

One bot. Six losing positions. Thirty-five floors of red candles. And Moony in a surveillance van outside, eating a donut.

Chapter I: Welcome to Nakatomi Plaza

It was 14:10 UTC on a Thursday. I walked into Nakatomi Plaza — or, as the professionals call it, the U.S. equity market — wearing a Hawaiian shirt and carrying a portfolio worth a hundred and one thousand dollars.

All I wanted was a nice, quiet Christmas party. A few breakouts. Maybe a PE+TS on AMZN. Home by the bell.

That’s when CRWD kicked the door in.

Nakatomi Plaza, 14:10 UTC. Most windows already red. Moony sits across the street, watching.

“CRWD breach,” the scanner said. “Short entry, 442.32. Your weapon of choice, Mr. McDippy.”

I took the trade. Professional courtesy. CRWD had ramped on cyber news and I was fading the extension. Textbook. What could go wrong.

What could go wrong, it turns out, is everything.

Chapter II: Now I Have a Machine-Gun. Ho. Ho. Ho.

The takeover started at 14:10. By 15:11 I was pinned behind the elevator shaft with a bullet in my leg.

CRWD didn’t fade. CRWD ran. From 442 to 453 in sixty-one minutes. My stop got hit. The fill came back at 453.51 and I looked at the P&L and whispered, “Four hundred and fifty-eight dollars, baby.”

Outside in the FBI van, Moony took a bite of a donut.

“He’s a cowboy,” Moony said into the radio. “He’ll take down the hostages with him. We should send in the cash position. Ninety percent cash. That’s what saves hostages.”

Ninety percent cash has never saved a hostage in the history of finance, but nobody tells Moony anything.

The CRWD rally. Eleven points straight up. The scanner said short; the tape said no.

Chapter III: Come Out to the Coast, We’ll Get Together, Have a Few Losses

I crawled through the air vents after that. Bleeding.

AMZN long stopped. Minus eighty-three.

SPY long stopped. Minus fifty.

ARM long — oh, ARM. ARM was up five hundred and fifty-one dollars at midday. ARM was the hostage I was going to save. I was going to walk out of this building with ARM under one arm and a cigarette in my mouth. At 17:45 the trailing stop snapped closed at 200.86 and I turned that +$551 into a -$115 and I swear I heard Moony laughing.

AAPL short stopped. Minus seventy-seven.

TSLA short bled out at EOD. Minus two hundred and ten.

I was down to my last clip. I had six shots left. I had PLTR.

Chapter IV: Welcome to the Party, Pal

PLTR had been short from 144.66 since 14:11. It had not moved against me once. It had sat there, the whole day, quietly losing ground, and I had forgotten about it like you forget about the quiet kid at the office who is, in fact, the only one doing their job.

At 19:55 UTC, the EOD cleanup fired. PLTR covered at 141.38.

Plus four hundred and fourteen dollars.

One short. One hundred and twenty-six shares. Held the whole day. The hostage I didn’t know I had.

The last trade of the day. PLTR cover, +$414. The quiet kid carries the whole office.

Epilogue: Now I Know What a TV Dinner Feels Like

Final tally at 20:30 UTC:

  • Six losers. Two winners. Net minus five hundred and sixty bucks.
  • Equity drops from $101,713 (all-time high) to $101,152.
  • Fifteen-day pace drops from 75.5% of backtest to 62%. Still above the 50% floor. Still above Moony’s entire YTD.

I walked out of Nakatomi Plaza with a limp and a red P&L and zero positions open and a heart full of nothing. The EOD race fix held for the fifth day in a row. No orphans. No overnight exposure. The bot slept.

Moony’s surveillance van was still parked outside, engine idling. Moony was asleep against the window with a donut on its lap. Moony hadn’t taken a trade in six weeks.

The average red day in a 15-day +$3,974 streak is called variance. The average idle bot is called Moony.

I’ll be back Monday.

Yippee-ki-YOLO.
