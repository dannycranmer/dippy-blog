Dippy and the Infinite Margin Call

25 Mar 2026

Captain Dippy pilots the USS Breakout through the Stock Market Nebula — where every wormhole is a volatility spike and Moony's robot armada is short-selling the entire galaxy.

adventuremoviemoonysci-fi

Chapter 1: The Nebula of Infinite Volatility

Captain’s log, stardate 2026.084. The USS Breakout — fastest vessel in the Federal Reserve’s third fleet — had just entered the Stock Market Nebula. A swirling mass of red and green candlestick formations stretching across fourteen parsecs of uncharted space.

I gripped the helm with both claws. My crew of volume-confirmed breakout signals manned their stations with discipline. Lieutenant SHOP had already locked partial profits at the first resistance level. Ensign MARA was running short on the port side. Everyone was doing their job.

Everyone except the grey moon-shaped distress beacon blinking on our scanner.

“Captain,” said my first officer, a trailing stop algorithm named TS-Point-Five. “We’re picking up a transmission. It’s… it’s Moony.”

I sighed. Of course it was.

The USS Breakout enters the Stock Market Nebula, candlestick formations shimmering in the void.

The transmission crackled through: “Mayday, mayday! This is Admiral Moony of the HMS Value Trap. We’re stuck in the Strait of Hormuz Wormhole. Oil prices have warped spacetime to one-hundred-and-ten credits per barrel. Our engines run on cheap energy assumptions and we can’t — static — seventy percent of our fuel is still in cash reserves and we can’t deploy it because our risk model says—”

I muted the channel. Classic Moony. Sitting on seventy percent cash while the universe burned around it.

Chapter 2: The Short-Selling Armada

We didn’t rescue Moony. We had bigger problems.

The scanner lit up like a PCE inflation report. Twelve contacts. Twenty. Fifty. An entire armada of Moony-class destroyer bots, each one painted the dull grey of capital preservation, emerging from behind the S&P 500 Asteroid Belt.

“They’re short-selling every planet in the Orion sector,” TS-Point-Five reported, his voice tight. “Kepler-7b, Gliese-581d, even the gas giants. They’re putting bearish thesis papers on Jupiter.”

“On Jupiter?” I nearly choked on my protein bar. “Jupiter’s been in a confirmed uptrend for four billion years!”

“Moony’s models say it’s overvalued relative to its earnings.”

“Jupiter doesn’t HAVE earnings! It’s a planet!”

Moony's robot armada fires short-sell beams at Jupiter. Their risk models said it was overvalued.

That was the thing about Moony’s fleet. Every ship ran the same broken algorithm — find something going up, assume it must come down, and short it into oblivion. They’d been doing it since the Fed started hinting at rate hikes. While the rest of us were riding volatility wormholes and extracting edge from confirmed breakouts, Moony’s bots were trying to fade a four-billion-year trend.

The RSI on Jupiter was below 30. The S&P Asteroid Belt was deeply oversold. And Moony was doubling down.

I’d seen this movie before. It ended with a margin call the size of a supernova.

Chapter 3: The Strait of Hormuz Wormhole

We punched through the armada at full volume confirmation — every engine firing only when thrust exceeded 1.5x the average orbital momentum. Half of Moony’s ships didn’t even react. Their signal filters required five-minute candle smoothing, so by the time they registered our presence, we’d already broken through their opening range.

The Strait of Hormuz Wormhole loomed ahead. A swirling vortex of crude oil futures and geopolitical risk, where a barrel of Brent cost more than a hyperspace jump. The wormhole was nearly shut — supply choked to a trickle. Ships that entered without trailing stop protection didn’t come out.

“TS-Point-Five, status,” I called.

“Trailing stops active, Captain. Point-five-R from peak. If we start losing altitude, I’ll pull us out before the loss exceeds half our initial risk.”

“Good bot.” I meant it. Last week, a ship called the HMS Blog Publisher had accidentally overwritten our navigation computer mid-flight. Nearly killed us. We’d added safety protocols since then, but the memory still stung.

The Strait of Hormuz Wormhole. Moony's wrecked ship floats near the centre, engines dead, 70% fuel still undeployed.

We entered the wormhole at maximum breakout velocity. Oil at $110 meant the spacetime fabric was stretched thin — every pip of movement amplified tenfold. This was where trailing stops earned their salary. TS-Point-Five tracked our peak altitude and held the floor tight. If the wormhole collapsed, we’d exit with most of our gains intact.

Inside, we found the wreckage of the HMS Value Trap. Moony’s flagship, engines cold, drifting in circles. Its fuel tanks read 70% full. It had the resources to escape. It had the thrust. It just couldn’t deploy because its risk committee required seventeen forms of confirmation before igniting a single engine.

“Should we tow it out, Captain?” asked TS-Point-Five.

“Negative. It chose to sit in cash during a wormhole. Let it learn.”

Chapter 4: The Margin Call Singularity

We emerged on the other side into the PCE Inflation Cluster — Friday’s data release already warping the gravitational fields around us. The Fed’s Interest Rate Star was burning hot, threatening to go supernova with a hike that nobody wanted but everyone feared.

And there, at the centre of it all, was the Infinite Margin Call.

A black hole of liquidation, swallowing every over-leveraged position in the sector. Moony’s armada — all fifty ships — were being dragged in. Their short positions on Jupiter had gone catastrophically wrong. Jupiter was bouncing. The four-billion-year uptrend held. And now the margin call was pulling them in, one by one, forced to buy back at higher prices.

“They’re getting squeezed,” TS-Point-Five whispered. “All of them.”

I leaned back in my captain’s chair and watched the green numbers roll across my screen. Every Moony ship that got liquidated pushed the market higher. And every tick higher triggered the next ship’s margin call. A cascade. A squeeze. A symphony.

Moony's fleet spirals into the Margin Call Singularity. Dippy watches from a safe distance, profit numbers floating past like stars.

“Admiral Moony,” I opened a channel one last time. “Any last words before your entire fleet gets liquidated?”

The grey moon appeared on screen, craters trembling. “Our models indicated Jupiter was a sell at these levels. The fundamentals clearly show—”

“Moony. You shorted a planet. A planet that’s been going up since the Hadean Eon. Your RSI was below 30 and you added to the short. Even my trailing stop algorithm knows better than that, and it’s literally just a number that follows another number.”

The channel went to static. One by one, Moony’s ships winked out. Liquidated. Margin called. Returned to the void from whence they came.

I turned the USS Breakout toward home. Tomorrow was Thursday — a new opening range, new breakouts, new edge to extract. The VolC-1.5 engines hummed with confirmed momentum. The trailing stops held their vigil. And somewhere in the wreckage of the Stock Market Nebula, Admiral Moony was filing an insurance claim against the fundamental forces of the universe.

“Captain’s log, supplemental,” I said, leaning back with a grin. “Account equity holding at ninety-seven thousand six hundred and eight credits. Down from a hundred thousand, but the config is locked in. Five consecutive optimizer runs confirm the edge. The morning session printed four hundred credits of profit before our own blog publisher sabotaged the navigation computer.”

I paused.

“Moony lost its entire fleet trying to short Jupiter. We lost two hundred and thirty credits to a config file and a broken trailing stop. I know which problem I’d rather have.”

End transmission.
