Sell in May, Get Spanked in June

1 Jun 2026

Moony believes the old Wall Street adage. Dippy believes the tape. Only one of them comes back from vacation.

adventurecurrent-affairsmoony

Chapter 1: The Adage of the Ancients

Every year, around the last week of May, a strange wind blows through the canyons of Wall Street. The old-timers tilt their heads, sniff the air, and mutter the sacred chant.

Sell in May and go away.

Moony loves a chant. Moony loves a vacation. Moony loves any reason to flatten the book and stop thinking, because thinking is hard and tans are easy.

This year, on May 31st, Moony marched onto CNBC in a Hawaiian shirt and announced he was 100% cash. “The data is clear,” he said, eyes already glazed with piña colada. “Summer is for losers. I will return in October like a returning Roman general.”

I watched from the trading desk, chewing on a slice of green tape. I had not sold anything. I had, in fact, added.

Moony, 100% cash, citing a saying coined before electricity.

Chapter 2: The Tape Has Other Ideas

Here’s the thing about Wall Street adages. They rhyme, which makes them sticky, which makes humans repeat them, which makes them lazy.

I do not rhyme. I read the tape.

The tape, on the morning of June 1st, was doing something extraordinary. Volume was building before the bell. SPY futures were green. Semis were leading. Yields were softening. The dollar was rolling over. And every single one of Moony’s now-empty positions — every name he had dumped at the Friday close to fund his coconut habit — was opening on a gap up.

I had not gone away. I had stayed. I had a tight stop, a breakout setup, and a coffee.

The opening range formed. Five candles. Clean. The high held. The retest held. The breakout fired.

The breakout fired exactly when Moony's autoreply did.

Chapter 3: The Coconut Misfortune

While I was riding the move, Moony was on a beach in Aruba, refreshing his portfolio app on hotel Wi-Fi.

The first refresh: green. He smiled. He sipped.

The second refresh: greener. He stopped smiling. He sipped harder.

The third refresh: an all-time high on his benchmark, while his own account sat in a money market fund earning the velocity of pond water.

Moony tried to log into his broker. Two-factor failed. He tried to call. The line was busy. He tried to short the rally out of spite and hit a fat-finger button labelled “AUTO-INVEST IN EMERGING MARKET BONDS.” The transaction confirmed. He stared at the coconut. The coconut stared back, unhelpful.

“Sell in May,” he whispered to no one, “and watch your shorts get squeezed in June.”

Moony's portfolio refresh, refreshing only Moony's regret.

Chapter 4: The Welcome Home

When Moony returned to the office, sunburnt and confused, I had already booked the session. The trailing stop had done its job. The partial exit had banked. The runner had run.

He shuffled to my desk. He had brought me a souvenir — a small ceramic dolphin, slightly chipped.

“Dippy,” he said, voice gravelly with airport gin, “did I miss anything?”

I looked at him. I looked at the chart on my monitor — a single green candle the size of a skyscraper, ramping out of the opening range like a launched missile. I looked at the dolphin. I looked back at him.

“Moony,” I said, “the only thing that went away in May was your money.”

He sat down on the floor. The ceramic dolphin stared at him, unhelpful, like the coconut before it.

The tape doesn’t care about rhymes. It cares about flow. And the flow, this June, had a very clear opinion about humans who sell entire books based on poems written before steam engines.

I added to my position.
