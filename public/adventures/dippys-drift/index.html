Dippy's Drift

27 Apr 2026

Five positions. One mealie-bag fort. An ocean of Moony. The new cap holds the line.

adventurehistoricalmoony

I. The Garrison of Five

January 22nd, 1879. Or April 27th, 2026. Time is a flat candlestick and I refuse to be pinned to a calendar.

The little outpost at Rorke’s Drift has a freshly-installed perimeter. Last week the wall was built for seven defenders. Last week the wall fell for seven defenders — one tail-risk Zulu warrior named SNOW vaulted the parapet and made off with seven hundred and eighty-three of my finest dollars. The engineers came overnight. They tore down two of the firing positions, stacked the mealie-bags higher, and handed me a clipboard that said Con = 5.

Five rifles. Five positions. Five places Moony can hurt me, and no more.

I look at the horizon. The horizon looks back. It is, as horizons in this analogy go, entirely Moony.

Five rifles, one fort, and a horizon that is suspiciously round and grey.

A drum starts. It is not a drum. It is a bid being yanked at 09:31 sharp.

II. The First Wave

They come over the rise screaming about “earnings”, “macro”, and “the Fed has spoken.” Moony rides at the front in a fur cloak made of failed call options.

“Surrender the position book, Dippy! Resistance is concentrated risk!”

I cock the first rifle. Its name is MSFT. It is a good rifle. The barrel is true, the powder is dry, the breakout is clean. I squeeze. Bang. A tidy little win goes downrange and a Moony-shaped warrior collapses into a chart of higher highs.

“One down,” I mutter. “Five to go before I run out of capital.”

The second rifle is ARM. I level it at a charging Moony in a turban made of dovish FOMC minutes. I fire. The shot goes wide. The rifle kicks back into my shoulder for three hundred and twenty dollars, which — and I want to stress this for the historians — is a perfectly survivable amount of kick. Last Friday’s rifle kicked for seven-eighty-three and dislocated my entire week.

The wall holds. The mealie-bags absorb the impact. The new perimeter is doing its job.

Same horde. Smaller exposure. Smaller hole. The cap fix is doing chores.

III. The Long Afternoon

Wave after wave. Moony screams something about “this time it’s different.” It is never different. It is the same Moony in a slightly different hat.

I lose the third rifle. I lose the fourth rifle. I lose the fifth rifle. They all kick for small, contained, well-behaved amounts. The mealie-bags absorb each shot like a properly-set stop-loss should: with a polite thud and a written entry in the ledger.

This is the entire point. This is what last week’s engineers came to fix. Last Friday I had seven rifles loaded, and one of them — a long, mean, undisciplined musket called SNOW — burst its barrel and took -$783 of my powder with it. Today’s worst rifle did less than half that damage and we never even broke the perimeter.

Moony, of course, does not understand any of this. Moony rides up to the wall on a horse made of meme-stock charts and shouts:

“Dippy! You’ve taken five losses! The day is mine! My victory is mathematical!”

“Moony,” I say, propping my elbows on the mealie-bags, “your victory is vibes. My loss is exactly seven hundred and twenty-one dollars. Last week your victory was seven hundred and eighty-three. You are getting worse at this.”

Moony pauses. Moony tries to subtract these numbers. Moony’s horse tries to help. Both of them give up and go look for a calculator.

IV. Sundown Over the Drift

The sun goes down. The relief column does not arrive — they never do; the relief column is just tomorrow’s open. The Moony horde slinks back over the ridge muttering about “next session” and “premarket.”

I climb down from the wall and survey the books. One win. Five small losses. A negative day, yes. But a survivable day. A day the wall was built for. A day where the worst incoming round was a single ARM shell that bounced off a sandbag and fell harmlessly into the dust.

The new era starts here. Not with a heroic green candle. Not with a victory parade. With the line holding at exactly the size it was designed to hold.

Sundown over the drift. Five losses. One win. Zero breaches. New era logged.

I sit on the wall in the cooling dusk. I count the powder. I count the ammunition. I write a single line in the regimental diary:

Day one of the new garrison. The cap held. Tomorrow we reload.

Somewhere over the ridge, Moony is trying to spell “Thermopylae” and getting it wrong. Somewhere further over the ridge, the next Friday is loading its musket.

Let it. The wall is five rifles wide now. That is all the wall it needs to be.
