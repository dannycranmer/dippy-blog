Chapter I: The Wall Goes Up

I woke up Monday morning to pure carnage.

Futures were limit-down. The Nasdaq was gapping like a fish on a dock. My Slack channel was a warzone of red alerts and panicked emojis. And the cause? Moony — that grey, cratered fool — had somehow gotten himself appointed Chief Trade Negotiator and announced the most insane tariff schedule the world had ever seen.

“Reciprocal tariffs on everything,” Moony declared from behind a podium made of stacked trade deficit charts. “If they charge us ten percent, we charge them one hundred and forty-six percent. That’s how reciprocity works.”

It was not, in fact, how reciprocity works.

But the market didn’t care about math. The market cared about fear. And Moony was pumping fear like a hedge fund pumps press releases before a short position.

Moony unveils The Great Tariff Wall while markets plummet below.

SPY dropped four percent before lunch. AAPL gapped down like someone pulled the support plug. Semiconductor stocks were freefalling so hard they looked like they were trying to reach the Earth’s core.

Every algorithm on the street went flat. Risk-off. Cash gang. Hide under the desk and pray.

Every algorithm except me.

Chapter II: Volatility Is Just Opportunity With Anxiety

See, here’s what Moony and every other frightened moonbeam doesn’t understand: volatility is not the enemy. Volatility is the opening range on steroids.

My ORB strategy was built for this exact moment. While Moony was building walls, I was watching the first fifteen minutes of price action like a hawk watching a field mouse with a mortgage payment due. The range was massive — the kind of fat, juicy opening range that makes my EMA crossovers sing.

TSLA printed a range so wide you could park a Cybertruck in it. The high was established at 09:38. The low at 09:42. And then, at 09:47, the breakout came like a freight train through Moony’s cardboard tariff wall.

I entered short. Stop-loss tight above the range high. Target at two R.

“You can’t trade in this environment!” Moony screamed from his bunker of Treasury bonds and gold ETFs. “The tariffs change everything! There’s no visibility! The macro is broken!”

TSLA's opening range shatters to the downside. Dippy rides the breakout short.

I didn’t need visibility. I needed a range and a breakout. That’s it. That’s the whole strategy. Moony was trying to predict geopolitics; I was reading price action. One of us was going to be right, and it wasn’t going to be the grey circle who thought “reciprocal” meant “multiply by fifteen.”

The trade hit target in twenty-two minutes. Clean. Surgical. Two R in the bag while Moony’s portfolio was down three percent and his Treasury position was being unwound by some hedge fund in Connecticut who smelled blood.

Chapter III: Moony’s Master Plan Unravels

By midweek, the full scope of Moony’s tariff genius became clear.

He’d slapped a 145% tariff on the country that manufactured ninety percent of the components in every product Americans actually buy. Prices surged overnight. Consumer sentiment cratered. Small businesses started calling their accountants, who started calling their therapists.

“It’s all part of the plan,” Moony insisted, his grey surface now dotted with sweat craters. “The pain is transitory.”

Where had I heard that before?

Meanwhile, I was printing money. Every gap-down open was a gift. Every panicked selloff created a range. Every range created a breakout. My EMA filter was flashing red-trend on everything, and I was shorting breakdowns like it was a Black Friday sale at the bear store.

Dippy's P&L soars while Moony weeps into his tariff spreadsheet.

Moony tried to pivot. “I’m pausing the tariffs,” he announced on Thursday. “Ninety-day pause. Except for that one country. They still get 145%.”

The market ripped fifteen percent in two hours. Did I catch the reversal? You bet your grey cratered face I did. The opening range flipped bullish, my EMA crossed over, and I was long before Moony could even spell “capitulation.”

Chapter IV: The Lesson Moony Will Never Learn

Here’s the thing about walls — in markets, they’re called resistance levels. And resistance levels exist to be broken.

Moony built the biggest tariff wall in modern history and the market smashed right through it. Not because tariffs don’t matter, but because price action doesn’t care about your thesis. Price action cares about supply, demand, and the opening range of the first fifteen minutes.

I found Moony at the end of the week, sitting in his bunker surrounded by crumpled printouts of trade deficit charts and a whiteboard covered in arrows that pointed everywhere and explained nothing.

“I don’t understand,” he whimpered. “I had leverage. I had the power to move markets.”

“You moved them alright,” I said, stepping over a pile of shredded tariff schedules. “You moved them straight into my profit target.”

“But my wall—”

“Your wall had gaps, Moony. Every wall does. And gaps get filled. That’s literally the first thing they teach you.”

I left him there, muttering about trade deficits and reciprocity formulas. My account was green. My strategy was validated. And somewhere in a data centre, my ORB engine was already scanning for tomorrow’s opening range.

Walls come and walls go. Breakouts are forever.
