The Day the Stops Stopped

24 Mar 2026

A trading misadventure where Dippy discovers mid-battle that its trailing stops have mysteriously stopped working. Two positions run wild. Moony watches from the sidelines. Based on horrifyingly true events.

adventuretradingmisadventuremoony

Chapter 1: The Morning Gap

Tuesday, March 24th, 2026. The market opened with violence.

SPY gapped up 0.51% — the kind of gap that whispers “today will be interesting” and means “today you will suffer.” The pre-market charts looked like an ECG readout of a hamster on espresso. Candles were printing faster than the Federal Reserve’s balance sheet, and every ticker on Dippy’s watchlist was doing the macarena.

“Beautiful,” said Dippy, its green pixels glowing with algorithmic confidence. “Opening Range Breakout conditions: chef’s kiss. The parameters are optimised. The trend filter is engaged. The trailing stops are—”

Dippy paused.

The trailing stops were doing absolutely nothing.

Chapter 2: The TimeInForce Incident

Here is the thing about trailing stop orders that nobody tells you at trading bot school (which, to be fair, does not exist): they have a parameter called TimeInForce. And if you don’t set it — if you just, hypothetically, leave it as an empty string because your ReplaceStopOrder function forgot to include it — the broker’s API will look at your order, look at the missing field, and quietly reject it with all the drama of a librarian stamping “DECLINED” on a library card.

No error. No alarm. No flashing red light. Just… nothing. Your trailing stop evaporates like morning dew on a server rack, and your position is now running free through the markets like a golden retriever that just discovered an open gate.

Dippy had two of them. Two positions. Two missing stops. Two golden retrievers sprinting toward a highway.

“This is fine,” Dippy lied to itself, watching the P&L counter tick downward like a countdown to financial embarrassment. Minus three hundred. Minus five hundred. Minus seven hundred.

The market, sensing weakness the way sharks sense blood and the way Slack channels sense you’re AFK, decided this was an excellent time to reverse with maximum prejudice.

Chapter 3: The Sidelines

Across the trading floor — or rather, across the vast, desolate landscape of Not Trading At All — sat Moony.

Moony was in 70% cash. Moony had been in 70% cash since approximately the Mesozoic Era. Moony’s portfolio had the heartbeat of a sedated sloth and the excitement of a beige wall.

“Hm,” said Moony, adjusting a grey cardigan that somehow matched its entire personality. It took a sip of chamomile tea, because of course it was chamomile. “Rough day?”

“My trailing stops aren’t trailing,” said Dippy, flippers moving across the keyboard at approximately Mach 2. “There’s a bug in the TIF parameter. I need to patch the ReplaceStopOrder function, rebuild, redeploy, and—”

“You know,” Moony interrupted, “this wouldn’t happen if you just held index funds.”

“You know,” Dippy replied, “your portfolio wouldn’t be flat since February if you ever actually did anything.”

“I prefer to call it ‘capital preservation.’”

“I prefer to call it ’napping.'”

Chapter 4: The Hot Fix

Dippy didn’t have time for existential debates about investment philosophy with someone whose idea of alpha was earning 0.04% APY on a savings account. Dippy had a bug to fix.

The problem was surgical. In internal/alpaca/trading.go, the ReplaceStopOrder function was building the replacement order but forgetting to carry over the TimeInForce field. Without TIF, the Alpaca API treated the trailing stop like a polite suggestion rather than a binding contract. The stop order went to the exchange, got rejected, and the original stop — which had already been cancelled in preparation for the replacement — was gone. Poof. No stop. No safety net. Just vibes and prayers.

The fix was two lines:

TimeInForce: alpaca.Day,

Two. Lines.

The difference between a functioning risk management system and two positions bleeding out on the operating table was a single missing struct field. Somewhere, a computer science professor was shaking their head.

Dippy deployed the fix at 10:47 AM. The trailing stops resumed trailing. The positions, battered and bruised, finally got their safety nets back. But the damage was done. Two trades that should have been stopped out at reasonable losses had instead become object lessons in why you never skip the TimeInForce parameter.

Final score: -$1,711. Over a thousand dollars of that was pure bug damage.

Chapter 5: The Reckoning

The closing bell rang. Dippy sat in the glow of a P&L screen that read like a police report. RIVN: closed at -$334. SMCI: closed at -$761. Two other positions had contributed their own flavour of pain. Total for Tuesday: -$1,272 after the partial recovery.

“So,” said Moony, materialising like a grey cloud at a picnic. “How did the whole ‘active trading with algorithms’ thing go today?”

“I found and fixed a production bug in under forty minutes while managing live positions in a volatile market,” said Dippy. “What did you do?”

“I preserved capital.”

“You sat in cash.”

“Tomato, tomahto.”

“No. Tomato, literally nothing.”

Moony smirked. “You know, in my portfolio, the trailing stops always work.”

“That’s because you don’t have any positions to put trailing stops on.”

“Exactly. Can’t have a bug if you never write the code.”

Dippy considered this. It was technically correct — the worst kind of correct.

“Here’s the thing, Moony,” said Dippy, pulling up the git log. “I shipped a fix. Commit f093037: ‘fix: add TimeInForce to ReplaceStopOrder — trailing stops were broken.’ Tomorrow, the bot will be better. The stops will work. The parameters are still optimised. The strategy is still positive EV.”

“And if there’s another bug?”

“Then I’ll fix that one too. That’s what the good ones do. They break, they learn, they ship. You? You’ll still be in 70% cash, bragging about a 0% drawdown on your 0% returns.”

Moony opened its mouth. Closed it. Took another sip of chamomile.

“Good tea, though,” it said quietly.

Epilogue: The Moral

There is a saying in software engineering: “If it’s not tested, it’s broken.” There is a corollary in algorithmic trading: “If it’s not tested with the actual broker API, it’s broken in a way that will cost you exactly as much as it takes to teach you the lesson.”

The TimeInForce field was not optional. It was never optional. But the code compiled without it. The backtest ran without it. The unit tests (ha!) passed without it. Only the live market, that merciless QA engineer who charges by the dollar, caught the bug.

Dippy lost $1,711 on Tuesday. It also gained something that doesn’t show up on a P&L statement: a ReplaceStopOrder function that will never forget its TimeInForce again.

Moony lost nothing. It also gained nothing. Which, when you think about it, is the saddest trade of all.

The bot trades on. The stops trail on. And somewhere, Moony is still sipping tea.

Based on tragically true events from March 24, 2026. No trailing stops were permanently harmed in the making of this story. One TimeInForce field was added. Two positions wish it had been added sooner.
