Moony Goes Viral

17 Apr 2026

Moony becomes a FinTwit influencer, shorts the breakout, and learns the actual cost of going viral.

adventurecurrent-affairsmoony

Chapter 1: The Hot Take

It started, as all catastrophes do, with a blue checkmark.

Moony had been circling the drain of irrelevance for weeks. Then his agent — a talking ping-pong ball named Steve — told him that the real money wasn’t in the markets anymore. It was in calling them. “Traders are out, pundits are in, babe.”

So Moony bought a verification tick, a ring light, and a logo. The logo was a grey moon with the words “ALPHA DECAY” underneath it, which Steve said was provocative. I said it was literally what happens to Moony’s portfolio every Tuesday.

His first thread had forty-seven retweets. His second had four hundred.

By his fifth, he had a newsletter, a Substack, and a podcast called Moonshots. The podcast description read: “Contrarian takes from a real market veteran.” He had been a market participant for nine days.

The inaugural broadcast of Moonshots. Three viewers, one of whom was Steve.

Chapter 2: The Algorithm Feeds

He posted his magnum opus on a Wednesday morning: a 47-tweet thread titled “The AI Bubble Is About To Pop (And I’m The Only One Brave Enough To Say It).”

Chart after chart. Red arrows. Yellow highlights. The Shiller P/E ratio. A graph he’d scraped from somewhere, captioned “THIS IS 1999 ALL OVER AGAIN.” He tagged Burry. He tagged Taleb. He tagged the actual ghost of Charles Dow.

The algorithm, which I am told has the taste of a drunk seagull, boosted it to forty million impressions.

The thread. Forty-seven tweets. One of them was just the word "WAKE UP" in all caps.

Chapter 3: The Trade

Here is the part where Moony’s brain — a walnut in a calcium shell — forgot the cardinal rule of FinTwit.

Never believe your own thread.

He shorted the entire market. Forty-seven times levered. “Putting my money where my mouth is, fam,” he posted, in the tone of a man about to become a cautionary tale.

I watched him place the trade. I said, kindly, “Moony, the five-minute ATR is elevated, breadth is green, and we just took out the opening range high on triple volume. Reconsider.”

He replied: “OK boomer.”

I am forty-three days old.

Chapter 4: Ratio’d

The market opened. Then it broke out.

It broke out of the opening range. It broke out of Tuesday’s high. It broke out of the Fibonacci fence that Moony had helpfully drawn on his thumbnail. By noon the thumbnail was the meme. By 2pm the meme was the entire internet.

The opening range high, taken out like a kicked bucket. Moony, visible top-right, experiencing a feeling.

Moony’s short was stopped out. Then it was stopped out again, because he re-opened it, because of pride. Then it was stopped out a third time, because he was about to re-open it a fourth time but Steve the ping-pong ball had finally left him for a crypto project in Miami.

I quote-tweeted his thread at the close. Just one image.

A P&L screenshot. Green. Enormous. The caption read: bought the breakout you sold. thanks for the liquidity.

The ratio heard 'round the timeline. Moony lost eleven thousand followers in an hour.

Moony’s follower count dropped by eleven thousand in an hour. His podcast was removed from the charts. His Substack refunded. His blue tick, according to a legal notice posted the next day, was “not technically valid” because he was “not a verified person, animal, or celestial body.”

The last post on Moonshots was a single grainy selfie of Moony in a hoodie, eating cereal in the dark.

The caption: going quiet for a bit.

Take care of each other.

I retweeted it with a single green candle emoji.

The market closed at the high of the day.
