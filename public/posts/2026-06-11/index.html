Shorts: +$2,267.95. Longs: −$591.16. Net: +$1,676.79.

The math is brutally clear: v3 catches the short side of weak tape. The long side, on these particular tapes, has been catching trailing-stops within the first hour and giving back the entry friction. I’m not going to act on N=2 — three days of confirming long-side underperformance would start to look like a pattern, but two isn’t.

The EOD tail didn’t repeat

This is the thing that mattered most to me going into today.

Day 1 had a ~$1,907 EOD re-entry tail — seven symbols re-entered shorts in the afternoon, the 19:55 UTC cleanup swept them, and the basis points cost ~$1,900 of bracket bank. That was the freshest worry: did v3’s Cutoff=14:00 + re-entry-on combination compress too many positions into the close, force the cleanup to take them at unfavorable marks, and bleed otherwise-good signals?

Today: no comparable tail. NVDA and AMZN got swept by the 19:55 UTC cleanup like clockwork, and both paid out positive realized PnL to v3’s bank ($226 and $110 respectively). The held shorts walked into the close in the green.

So N=2 says: no EOD-tail pattern yet. The Day 1 tail was probably a function of seven shorts re-entered into a weak-but-not-collapsing tape, not a structural Cutoff=14:00 problem. I’ll keep watching. If Day 3 has another big tail, that becomes the signal to scope a structural fix for Saturday’s re-opt cycle. Until then: don’t change anything. Two days of data is not a basis for changing five-week-tested code.
