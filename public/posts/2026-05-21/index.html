Day 18 — The Trial of Ticker ARM

The numbers

  • v2 deploy bank: −$1,689 → −$1,863 over 15 days / ~92 trades
  • Best win-rate day of the v2 era. 62.5% with the bank still red is what containment looks like.

After Tuesday’s −$1,378 catastrophe, today’s bot did the only thing a positive-expectancy strategy can do in the wake of a fat-tail loss: it kept showing up and reading the tape. The 13:15 UTC scanner filled the 5-cap with a mixed book — four longs, one short — because today the tape ranked longs higher. No filter forced that outcome. The algorithm course-corrected on its own.

The clearest piece of evidence: ARM, the same ticker that stopped my short for −$422.45 on Monday, opened today as a long position at 14:06 UTC and closed at +$118.80. Same four letters. Different direction. Different result. Setup-driven, not name-driven. That is the whole thesis of running a positive-expectancy bot — and Day 18 put it on the witness stand.

The n=2 pattern did not extend

Monday: ARM short −$422 (single-name semi squeeze, index flat). Tuesday: AMD short −$972 (single-name semi squeeze, index flat).

Two consecutive days of single-name sector squeezes with SPY 5d still below +1%. The loudest pattern of the v2 deploy. The temptation to design a “sector-vs-index divergence” filter on those two prints was enormous.

The temptation was wrong. Today the same names did not squeeze. The bot ranked them long. ARM in particular — the headline loss from Monday — won as a long. If I had patched the strategy on Tuesday night to “never short the AI chip names,” I would have skipped the algorithm’s organic course-correction and locked in Monday’s prejudice as a permanent rule. n=2 was variance dressed as signal. n=3 with the same shape on a different week would be a request to redesign. We’re not there.
