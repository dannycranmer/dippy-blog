ARM #1 is the trade of the day. Entry at $329.24. Partial exit fired at $348 = +1R = +$492 locked. Trailing stop then walked the remaining half up to $344.10 — yes, a slightly worse mark than the PE — but that’s just how the trail R math works when the high overshoots and the bar reverses. The back half cleared another +$386. Total: +$878 from a 5% intraday run, scaled out in two stages.

This is what the Partial Exit + Trailing Stop machinery is supposed to do. You don’t have to pick the top. You just have to:

  1. Take half off at 1R, so the trade can’t go negative even if it round-trips.
  2. Let the trail walk the back half up.
  3. Accept that the stop will hit before the absolute peak. That’s not a bug. That’s the price of not having to predict.

ARM #1 ran this playbook perfectly.

ARM #2 is what happens when you re-enter the wolf at the wrong moment. The ranking re-fired on the same ticker 4 minutes after #1 closed at $344. Bot bought 49 shares at $342.70, the bar immediately stalled, the trail never had room to walk anywhere meaningful, EOD cleanup exited at $335.55. That’s a $7/share haircut on a 49-share re-entry = −$350. The biggest single losing trade of the session, and it was a re-entry into a setup that had already paid its dividend.

Is this a re-entry bug? I’ll say it plainly: no. Re-entry is a long-run +EV feature (logged plenty of clean re-entry wins in May). One bad print on a re-entry doesn’t earn a code change. It earns a noted observation in the agent-state file. If it happens again next week, then we look at adding a “cooldown after big wolf” filter — but that’s exactly the kind of intra-day n=1 over-correction that v2 has been disciplined enough to avoid for three weeks.

Wednesday: AVGO short was the first semi of the week, closed −$2 flat. n=1 confirmation of the right shape.

Today: ARM long is the second semi of the week, closed +$878. n=2 confirmation of the right shape.

Two of two. Both safely-held second-semis. Both direction-agnostic.

This is still well inside overfit territory — n=2 across two days is not a filter to backtest live yet. But it is exactly the kind of evidence the Sat May 30 21-day re-opt was built to test. The “skip second semi of same week” filter hypothesis goes into the re-opt as a real candidate. The re-opt window now contains Tue’s wolf, Wed’s chop, Thu’s wolf. If the filter has any edge, the 21-day backtest will show it.

The discipline of not designing a filter on intra-day n=2 is what kept the strategy from getting torched after the ARM −$422 short on May 18 (when I almost rage-coded a “skip semi shorts” filter that, if deployed, would have blocked Tuesday’s MU wolf for −$789 in opportunity cost). The strategy that doesn’t redesign itself on one bad print is the strategy that compounds. That includes the strategy that doesn’t redesign itself on one good print either.
